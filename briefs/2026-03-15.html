Section 01

Markets

Stagflation Is No Longer a Hypothetical
Friday’s double data release made it official. Q4 GDP was revised down to 0.7% annualized—sharply below earlier estimates and the weakest quarter since 2022. Core PCE printed 3.1% YoY with a 0.4% monthly pace, well above the Fed’s comfort zone. Growth decelerating while inflation remains sticky. The stagflation scenario that markets dismissed six weeks ago is now embedded in the data, and it doesn’t yet include the oil shock.
The Failed Rally
Markets tried. Dow opened up 300 points on PCE relief—headline came in at 2.8%, a tick below estimates. But by afternoon, Kharg Island headlines overwhelmed the bid. S&P –0.61% to 6,632. Nasdaq –0.93%. For the week: S&P –1.6%, Dow –2%, Nasdaq –1.3%—the first three-week losing streak in about a year. Every attempted rally this month has been sold. The buyers aren’t gone, but they can’t hold anything overnight.
Adobe’s Signal
ADBE –8.5% Friday after reporting Thursday night. The numbers beat, but CEO Shantanu Narayen announced his departure after 18 years and recurring subscription guidance disappointed. ULTA –8% on weak forward guidance despite a beat. The pattern: companies beating estimates but getting punished for cautious outlooks. The market is paying for certainty and selling ambiguity.
Gold Breaks Down
GFI –10.60%, AEM –4.89%. GLD –1.29%, SLV –4.93%. The precious metals selloff was the session’s surprise—gold typically rallies in geopolitical crises. But dollar strength and rising real rates are overwhelming the safe-haven bid. The safe haven trade has rotated from gold to energy and utilities.

Section 02

Geopolitics

The War Went Regional Over the Weekend
Saturday brought the broadest escalation since the war began. Iran launched 9 ballistic missiles and 33 drones at the UAE. Debris from an intercepted drone hit the Fujairah oil port, suspending some loading operations. Drones struck a US air base in Kuwait, injuring three service members. The US Embassy compound in Baghdad took a direct hit. Iran’s IRGC declared UAE ports—Jebel Ali, Khalifa, Fujairah—legitimate targets. This is no longer bilateral—it’s a regional war touching six countries.
Trump’s Naval Coalition Gambit
Saturday, Trump called on China, France, Japan, South Korea, and the UK to send warships to keep Hormuz open. Neither Beijing nor London confirmed. Iran’s IRGC navy chief fired back: the Strait is “not militarily closed, merely under control.” The framing is deliberate—Iran is selectively allowing Indian, Turkish, and some Chinese vessels through while blocking Western-allied shipping. This is economic warfare with surgical precision, not a blanket blockade.
Ceasefire Rejected
Reuters reported Saturday that Trump rejected efforts to launch ceasefire talks. He’s betting on military pressure to force unconditional surrender. Iran’s foreign minister said Tehran will target any regional energy infrastructure belonging to American companies if its own oil facilities are struck. 5,000 additional Marines deploying. USS Nimitz decommissioning postponed. The off-ramp exists, but neither side is taking it.

If operations resume, the market may hold $103. Either way, the weekend escalation to six countries changes the risk premium.

If Huang delivers a demand narrative strong enough to decouple semis from oil—think $700B annual capex cycle framing—our names could bounce 5–10% this week. If GTC underwhelms, the oil narrative wins and semis test new lows.

If the median dot shifts to zero cuts for 2026, equities reprice immediately. If it holds at one–two cuts with dovish growth language, relief rally. If this persists, the post-war energy order looks fundamentally different. Structurally bullish for US energy independence names.
6. The binary outcome window narrows this week. Trump rejected ceasefire talks and issued the Kharg oil ultimatum. Iran is retaliating across six countries. The middle ground of $100 oil with a controlled standoff is collapsing. Either a framework materializes—oil drops $20–30, equities explode higher—or the escalation spiral continues.
