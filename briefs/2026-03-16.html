Section 02

Geopolitics

Day 17 — The Hormuz Coalition Takes Shape
Trump spent the weekend pressing allies to send warships to force Hormuz open, threatening NATO with “very bad” consequences for non-compliance. No country has publicly committed. Australia and Japan both declined Monday. The Wall Street Journal reported Trump aims to announce a “Hormuz Coalition” later this week, with Japan’s PM Takaichi visiting the White House Thursday.
The Two-Tier Strait
The strategic picture has shifted in one critical way: the US is now allowing Iranian oil tankers through the Strait. Bessent confirmed it—“the Iranian ships have been getting out already, and we’ve let that happen to supply the rest of the world.” This creates an extraordinary dynamic: Iran exports its own crude freely to China and non-aligned buyers while blocking Western-allied shipping. It’s a de-dollarization play inside a kinetic war.
Khamenei Reportedly in Moscow
Unconfirmed reports from Kuwaiti media claim Mojtaba Khamenei was secretly airlifted to Moscow for leg surgery after injuries sustained in the February 28 strikes. Putin reportedly offered personally. US Defense Secretary Hegseth said Friday the new supreme leader is “wounded and likely disfigured.” If the IRGC is operating autonomously without clear civilian authority, the war’s escalation logic becomes less predictable—but a ceasefire also becomes harder to negotiate.
The Reserve Clock
A Pakistan-flagged tanker transited the Strait Sunday, hugging Iran’s coast—a trickle of commerce in what used to be the world’s busiest oil corridor. The 572M barrel buffer started depleting March 12. At current disruption levels, depletion hits mid-April. If the coalition doesn’t materialize or a ceasefire doesn’t emerge, Brent goes to $120+ when reserves run dry.
