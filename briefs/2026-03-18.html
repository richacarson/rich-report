Markets

Equities Grind Higher Into the FOMC
Tuesday closed green across the board—S&P +0.26% to 6,708 (SPY $670.79), Nasdaq +0.49%, Russell +0.45%, Dow +0.13%. UVXY –1.74%. The advance was narrow and grinding, not the kind of broad thrust you see at inflection points. But it’s two consecutive green days—the first back-to-back gains since before the Hormuz blockade. Equities are choosing to look through the oil spike and toward this afternoon’s Fed decision. The question is whether Powell validates that optimism or crushes it.
Oil Rips Back Above $100 WTI
Monday’s oil pullback lasted exactly one session. WTI surged +3.31% to ~$100+, Brent +3.65% to ~$106. The catalysts: a projectile hit near Iran’s Bushehr nuclear plant (Russia’s Rosatom condemned it), a drone struck the US embassy compound in Baghdad, and Iran announced a “new protocol” for the Hormuz Strait—language that signals tighter, not looser, transit controls. Shipping insurance costs to cross Hormuz have soared per Bloomberg. The de-escalation trade from Sunday is dead. Energy led sectors at +1.05%, with VLO +1.46%, OKE +1.45%, and CVX +0.57% all catching the bid.
Semis Extend the GTC Momentum
The GTC bid carried into Day 2. LRCX led IOWN at +3.22%—now +6.7% in two sessions. TSM +1.69%, QCOM +1.70%, ADI +0.88%, NXPI +1.11%. NVDA itself faded –0.70% to $181.93 after Monday’s run, a classic “sell the news on the headliner, buy the ecosystem” rotation. The supply chain names are outperforming the platform company—consistent with a maturing AI capex cycle where picks and shovels matter more than the brand on the datacenter. KEYS launched an AI inference emulation platform at OFC 2026 and advanced ultra-ethernet interoperability demos—physical AI infrastructure materializing in real time.
The Fed at 2 PM ET
This is the event. A hold at 3.50–3.75% is certain (92%+ probability). The dot plot is the only variable that matters. Three scenarios: (1) median holds at one cut with language acknowledging the oil supply shock—equities rally, bonds rally, dollar weakens; (2) median shifts to zero cuts—bonds sell hard, 200DMA test resumes within days; (3) surprise hawkish dissents citing energy-driven inflation—worst case. Powell’s presser will be parsed for any signal on whether the Fed views the oil shock as “transitory” or structurally inflationary. He has two meetings left before Warsh succeeds him in May.

Section 02

Geopolitics

Day 19 — Decapitation Reaches the Intelligence Core
Israel confirmed overnight it killed Esmail Khatib, Iran’s Intelligence Minister, adding him to a growing list: Khamenei Sr. (Day 1), IRGC chief Salami, Larijani (Day 18), and now Khatib. The systematic elimination of Iran’s leadership layer has moved from political to military to intelligence. Separately, Iran executed a man accused of spying for Israel—a sign the regime is turning inward, hunting leaks that enable the strikes. The decapitation campaign is working operationally—someone inside the Iranian state is feeding targeting data in near-real-time.
Bushehr and Baghdad—Escalation on Two Fronts
A projectile hit near Iran’s Bushehr nuclear plant—no damage or casualties, but Russia’s Rosatom condemned the strike on what it considers Russian-supplied infrastructure. An Australian report confirmed an Iranian projectile struck a UAE airbase; all personnel safe. And a drone hit the US embassy compound in Baghdad. The war is no longer Israel-Iran bilateral—it’s pulling in Iraq, UAE, and potentially Russia as stakeholders. Iran’s statement that its “nuclear doctrine is unlikely to change” is meant to reassure but reads as a ceiling: Iran won’t go nuclear, but it won’t stop fighting either.
Hormuz “New Protocol”—Tighter, Not Looser
Iran announced the Strait “needs a new protocol,” rejecting the status quo ante. This kills the narrative that bilateral tanker deals would gradually normalize traffic. Tehran is signaling it views Hormuz control as a permanent lever, not a temporary wartime tactic. Combined with soaring shipping insurance costs, this structurally reprices every barrel of Gulf crude. Trump slammed NATO allies for not joining the war effort but said the US “never needed their help.” Japan’s PM is in Washington today with Hormuz at the top of the agenda. Sweden’s security service named Russia, China, and Iran as the three main threats—the war is reshaping European threat assessments in real time.
