Gold Carnage Deepens — This Is Forced Liquidation
Gold crashed –4.12% to $426 (GLD). Silver –4.40%. This is the second consecutive day of violent precious metals selling during an active shooting war—a textbook forced-liquidation pattern. AEM –5.77%, GFI –4.72%. When the ultimate safe haven sells harder than equities during a geopolitical crisis, the message is clear: margin calls are overwhelming fundamentals. The dollar is weakening simultaneously (Reuters: “Dollar slips, bonds struggle as Iran war spurs hawkish rate rethink”), which should be gold-positive. It isn’t, because leveraged positions must be unwound regardless of thesis. The snap-back when this exhausts will be violent in the other direction.

US pump prices are up 30% since the war began, headed toward $4/gallon. Morgan Stanley pushed rate cut expectations to September and December—the market is repricing for a prolonged energy shock, not a quick resolution. Global Trade Rerouting Accelerates
Reuters reports US crude is now heading to Asia via the Panama Canal as the Iran crisis redraws global trade flows. This is not a temporary adjustment—it’s the beginning of a structural shift in energy logistics. The EU is eyeing energy tax cuts and subsidies to cushion the blow; India’s rupee breached 93 per dollar to hit a record low. The second-order effects of the Hormuz disruption are proliferating: European chip buyers are paying more and tapping backup stores as air freight costs spike (CNBC, re: NXPI). The UAE dismantled a “terrorist network” funded by Iran and Hezbollah—Gulf states are hardening their posture. Every week this conflict continues, the permanent infrastructure changes deepen.
Netanyahu’s Post-War Energy Vision

That vision requires sustained conflict to make the Hormuz route unviable long enough to force permanent rerouting. MATX +1.33% (shipping), OKE +2.55% (midstream), and the broader US energy logistics chain are the beneficiaries of a world where Gulf transit risk is permanently elevated. Physical world matters.

On Our Radar

1. Fear & Greed at 11 is a contrarian signal — but not yet an actionable one. Cramer is already saying “hold your nose and buy” based on oversold oscillators. He may be right eventually, but sentiment extremes can persist for weeks during active wars. The 2022 low hit Fear & Greed 6 before reversing. We’re at 11. The forced-liquidation pattern (gold, crypto, equities all selling) suggests the mechanical selling isn’t done. Our discipline: wait for the –20% trigger at ~5,600 or a clear technical reversal above the 200DMA at ~6,608. Don’t let sentiment indicators override price levels.
2. Paper vs. physical oil divergence is the most important signal in markets. Brent futures fell –1.96% but physical cargo prices set all-time highs. This happens when financial players de-leverage positions while real-world supply is genuinely constrained. When the de-leveraging ends, futures will re-converge with physical—violently upward. VLO, CVX, and OKE are positioned for this. The 30% pump price increase since the war began is flowing directly to refiner margins. Research Reveals Opportunities.
3. FDX beat is a positive logistics signal — but BABA miss clouds China. FedEx posted $5.25 vs. $4.16 estimate—a 26% beat. In a market starved for earnings visibility beyond oil, this matters: global parcel demand is holding despite rerouting costs. BABA at $6.96 vs. $11.88 est. is a disaster—Chinese consumer spending is weaker than even bears expected. PDD –3.27% dragged down in sympathy. Our China-adjacent names (FINV –4.93%, PDD –3.27%) are feeling this. ATAT +4.11% is the exception—domestic travel resilience diverging from e-commerce weakness.
4. LRCX is the GTC trade that keeps working. +4.13% Thursday, +11.3% since Monday. The stock broke through its 50-day, then its 100-day, and is now testing the February consolidation zone at $235. ADI +0.60%, TEL +1.09%, QCOM +0.62%—the AI picks-and-shovels thesis is absorbing the broader market selloff. Lam is now the top-performing IOWN holding over the past week. If it holds $230 on any pullback, the technical structure flips bullish. Think Like an Owner.
5. Gold miners approaching generational entry if forced selling exhausts. AEM has dropped from $208 to $185 in a week—an 11% drawdown during a war that should be gold-positive. GFI from $46 to $41. The thesis hasn’t changed: gold miners print cash at $2,600+/oz spot. The selling is mechanical (margin calls, CTA liquidation), not fundamental. When it stops, these names snap back hardest because the underlying commodity thesis is intact. AEM’s $178–$180 support from December is the line. We’re $5 above it.
6. Morgan Stanley pushing rate cuts to September changes the rate calculus. The market entered March pricing two cuts in 2026. Now MS sees September at earliest, with December as the second. This is a direct consequence of $110+ Brent repricing inflation expectations. TLT bounced +0.62% Thursday after selling for three straight sessions, but the direction of travel is fewer cuts, longer. ADP +1.14% and NEE +1.59% both outperformed—quality yield names with pricing power are the right place to be when rates stay higher for longer. Simplicity Over Complexity.
