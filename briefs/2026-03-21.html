Weekend Assessment — De-Leveraging Enters Final Phase
Friday’s session closed the worst week for gold since September 2011—down 9.6% to $4,495. Silver fell 14% on the week. Brent settled at $112.19, its highest war-era close, even as paper futures whipsawed intraday between $108 and $119. The S&P closed at 6,598 (SPY $659.80), holding the prior session’s level—equities are stabilizing while commodities and precious metals remain chaotic. Fear & Greed at 11 for a second day. TLT +0.62% suggests some flight to duration, but the bond market is conflicted: Morgan Stanley’s September rate cut timeline implies no relief from the energy-driven inflation pass-through. The IEA called this “the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market.” That is not hyperbole—Hormuz flows have dropped from 20 mb/d to a trickle.
Gold’s 9.6% Weekly Drop Is the Mechanical Clearing We Flagged
Thursday’s brief identified the gold selloff as forced liquidation. Friday confirmed it at scale. Analysts at SP Angel attribute the collapse to momentum trades unwinding after gold’s pre-war run to $5,600 in January. AEM ended the week at $184.78 (–5.77% Friday alone), GFI at $40.75 (–4.72%). The underlying thesis remains intact: J.P. Morgan forecasts $5,055/oz by Q4, Goldman sees $5,400/oz by December. UBS views this as “normal volatility within a continuing structural uptrend.” The question for Monday is whether the mechanical selling has exhausted—gold is non-trading this weekend, so Monday’s open could gap in either direction depending on conflict developments.
Brent Closes at War High Despite Intraday Volatility
Brent’s $112.19 close was its highest since the war began, even though intraday action saw a spike to $119 and a reversal. The catalyst: Netanyahu stated Israel is helping the U.S. open the Strait of Hormuz, which briefly calmed futures. But physical market reality diverges sharply—Dubai crude hit an all-time high above $150, Oman crude above $152. The $50+ gap between WTI ($96) and Dubai crude is unprecedented. The IEA’s 400-million-barrel emergency reserve release and the U.S. SPR tap of 172 million barrels have done little to contain prices. Goldman Sachs suggested elevated prices could persist through 2027. VLO +1.51%, CVX +1.42%, OKE +2.55% on Friday—energy infrastructure continues to outperform.

Section 02

Geopolitics

Day 22 — Natanz Hit, Diego Garcia Retaliated, Trump Floats ‘Winding Down’
Saturday brought the most consequential escalation yet: Iran confirmed its Natanz nuclear facility was struck by Israeli airstrikes, though Tehran said there has been no radiation leakage. Hours later, Iran fired missiles at the joint U.S.-U.K. Diego Garcia military base in the Indian Ocean—a significant expansion of the conflict’s geographic scope. An F-35 made an emergency landing after being struck by what is believed to be Iranian fire. Israeli Defense Minister Katz stated attacks on Iran will “increase significantly” in the coming week. The military trajectory is clearly escalatory.
Trump’s Contradictory Signals
Against this backdrop, Trump said the administration is considering “winding down” military efforts—while simultaneously deploying 2,200–2,500 additional Marines to the region. A senior Iranian source told CNN that Tehran does not believe Trump’s claim. The domestic political constraint is tightening: Americans expect ground troops but do not support it. If this is genuine off-ramp rhetoric, oil could pull back sharply on any ceasefire signal. If it is not, the troop deployments suggest escalation continues regardless. For Monday positioning, this ambiguity is the single most important variable.
Regional Spread Accelerates
Kuwait reported Iranian drones struck the Mina Al-Ahmadi refinery overnight, igniting fires. Iran warned of “crushing blows” against Ras al-Khaimah if further aggression is launched from UAE territory. Bahrain has now intercepted 143 missiles and 242 drones since February 28. The Lebanon death toll has surpassed 1,000 since Israeli attacks escalated on March 2. The conflict is no longer bilateral—it is a regional war involving at least six countries’ territory. Every new front complicates any ceasefire framework and extends the timeline for Hormuz normalization. NXPI exposure to air freight disruption and chip supply rerouting is a direct second-order risk for the portfolio.
