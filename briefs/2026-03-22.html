Section 01

Markets — Week in Review

The Week That Broke Correlations
The week ending March 21 produced a rare environment in which equities, bonds, gold, and crypto all declined while crude oil surged. The S&P 500 fell roughly 4.2% on the week, its fourth consecutive weekly decline. Brent crude rose approximately 8.7% to $112. Gold lost over 10% on the week to $4,490—its sharpest weekly decline in years—as forced liquidation overwhelmed the safe-haven bid. Bitcoin slid through the weekend to $68,951, moving below its recent multi-week trading range. Fear & Greed sits at 10, deep in Extreme Fear territory. The week’s dominant theme was clear: energy-driven inflation with no policy relief in sight. The Fed’s hawkish hold on Wednesday—with CME FedWatch now pricing 12% odds of an April hike and 48% odds of zero cuts this year—removed the last hope of near-term monetary easing. For IOWN, the energy sleeve (CVX, OKE, VLO) provided ballast while rate-sensitive holdings (NEE, BKH) and the digital asset sleeve absorbed the heaviest drawdowns.
When every asset class sells except crude oil, the market is expressing a single view: the energy shock is the macro. Monday’s open hinges entirely on whether Trump’s 48-hour ultimatum produces a Hormuz breakthrough or a new phase of escalation. Avoid Erosion—discipline over impulse in binary environments.
Section 02

Geopolitics

Trump’s 48-Hour Ultimatum — Deadline Monday 7:44 PM ET
Saturday night, Trump posted on Truth Social that if Iran does not fully reopen the Strait of Hormuz within 48 hours, the United States would strike Iranian power plants. Bloomberg and Al Jazeera both characterize this as the most consequential US ultimatum since the conflict began. The deadline falls at approximately 7:44 PM ET Monday—after the US equity close but before Asian markets open. This came less than 24 hours after Trump suggested the US was considering “winding down” military efforts, a whiplash that underscores the difficulty of pricing geopolitical risk in this environment. Iran has approximately 400 power plants, including the Bushehr nuclear facility. Tehran’s Khatam al-Anbiya military command responded that any attack on Iranian energy infrastructure would trigger retaliation against all US-linked energy, IT, and desalination infrastructure across the region.
Iran’s Selective Blockade — A New Hormuz Regime Emerges
While Trump frames Hormuz as fully closed, the reality is more nuanced. Iran is transitioning from a total blockade to a selective one, developing a vetting and registration system for ships. Al Jazeera reports Japanese ships may soon be permitted to transit after Foreign Minister Araghchi stated the strait is closed only to ships belonging to countries that attack Iran. Lloyd’s List reports at least 10 ships have transited via a safe corridor hugging Iran’s coastline. India, Pakistan, China, Malaysia, and Iraq are in direct talks with Tehran. An Iranian lawmaker indicated parliament is preparing legislation to levy transit fees—one tanker reportedly paid $2 million for passage. If Iran can monetize and selectively control the strait, the binary open-or-closed framing may not hold. This complicates Trump’s ultimatum: Iran could argue the strait is already open to non-combatants while maintaining leverage over Western-linked cargoes. For IOWN, the physical-versus-paper oil divergence (Dubai crude above $150 versus WTI near $98) reflects this selective reality. MATX exposure to shipping disruption and NXPI exposure to rerouted supply chains are second-order risks worth monitoring.
Saturday’s Escalation — Dimona, Diego Garcia, and Diplomatic Hardening
Iranian missiles struck Dimona and Arad in southern Israel on Saturday, wounding over 100 people. Dimona sits approximately 13 kilometers from Israel’s Negev Nuclear Research Center—the IAEA said it was aware of reports but had no indication of damage to the facility. Iran framed the strikes as direct retaliation for Israel’s hit on Natanz. Separately, Iran fired missiles at the joint US-UK Diego Garcia base in the Indian Ocean, though the UK reported the attack was unsuccessful. On the diplomatic front, Saudi Arabia expelled Iran’s military attaché and four embassy staff—a notable break from Riyadh’s earlier neutrality. Twenty-two nations issued a joint statement condemning the Hormuz blockade. Admiral Brad Cooper, head of US Central Command, stated that Iran’s ability to attack vessels in the strait has been degraded after US jets struck an underground coastal facility storing antiship cruise missiles. The conflict has expanded to at least six countries’ territory, and Israeli Defense Minister Katz stated that strikes on Iran would increase significantly this week.

If US strikes follow, Tehran has explicitly promised regional infrastructure retaliation—which could widen the energy supply shock. If Iran offers even a partial concession on Hormuz access, the relief trade could be significant: oil lower, equities higher, gold could stabilize on reduced liquidation pressure. The contradiction between Trump’s ultimatum and Iran’s emerging selective-access framework is the core tension. GD and LMT provide defense exposure; CVX, VLO, and OKE remain positioned for prolonged energy disruption. Think Like an Owner—we prepare for both outcomes without positioning around ambiguity.

4. Iran’s transit fee proposal could reshape global shipping economics. If Tehran’s parliament passes legislation charging for Hormuz passage, it would create a permanent toll on roughly 20% of global oil transit. The reported $2M-per-tanker fee, if standardized, would add meaningful cost to every barrel flowing through the strait—even after the conflict ends. An Iranian adviser to the supreme leader stated that a new regime for the Strait of Hormuz would follow the war’s eventual end. This is a structural shift worth monitoring for IOWN’s energy and shipping exposure. Simplicity Over Complexity.
