Markets

Asia Triggers Circuit Breakers — Kospi Halted, Nikkei Down 5%
The global week opened with a cascading Asian selloff. South Korea’s Kospi plunged more than 6%, triggering a circuit breaker suspension on Kospi 200 futures—a mechanism last invoked during the August 2024 yen unwind. Japan’s Nikkei 225 fell nearly 5%, Hong Kong’s Hang Seng shed 2.7%, and Taiwan’s Taiex declined 2.2%. The catalyst: Trump’s Saturday night ultimatum, which expires tonight at approximately 7:44 PM ET. S&P 500 futures are pointing to a gap-down open near 6,489, roughly 1.1% below Friday’s close. The index is now testing the lower boundary of its March descending channel. If the 200-day moving average fails to hold as support, algorithmic selling could accelerate the move lower. UVXY surged 10.5% on Friday and overnight activity suggests volatility compression remains unlikely this week.
Brent Pushes to $114 as Physical-Paper Divergence Widens
Brent crude climbed to approximately $114 in early Monday trading, extending Friday’s war-era closing high of $112.19. WTI rose above $100. The physical market continues to diverge sharply from futures: Dubai crude remains above $150, Oman crude above $152, while WTI paper trades near $100. The IEA has characterized this as the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market, with Hormuz flows reduced from 20 mb/d to a trickle over the past 19 days. Goldman Sachs warned that Brent could exceed its all-time high near $147 if disruptions lengthen, and suggested elevated prices may persist through 2027 in their base case. VLO, CVX, and OKE—IOWN’s energy infrastructure names—continue to provide portfolio ballast, with OKE gaining 0.93% on Friday against broad market weakness. The energy sleeve thesis is being validated in real time.
Gold Stabilizes Near $4,489 — Forced Liquidation May Be Exhausting
After last week’s 10.5% decline, gold opened Monday near $4,489—essentially flat from Friday’s close. The stabilization is notable given the continued escalation in geopolitical risk. If the mechanical selling that drove last week’s rout has indeed exhausted, gold could begin to reflect its safe-haven fundamentals again. J.P. Morgan’s Q4 target of $5,055 and Goldman’s $5,400 year-end forecast remain in place. AEM and GFI, which absorbed significant drawdowns last week, could see relief if gold finds a floor here. The dollar remains strong, which is the near-term headwind, but if the conflict intensifies further tonight, the flight-to-safety bid may reassert itself.

Section 02

Geopolitics

The Ultimatum — 7:44 PM ET Tonight
Trump’s 48-hour deadline for Iran to fully reopen the Strait of Hormuz expires tonight at approximately 7:44 PM ET—after the US equity close but before Asian markets open for Tuesday’s session. Iran’s response has been unambiguous: the IRGC stated that if the US strikes Iranian power plants, the strait will be “completely closed” and will not reopen until any destroyed infrastructure is rebuilt. Iran’s approximately 400 power plants include the Bushehr nuclear facility. The contradiction at the core of this standoff remains: Iran is already operating a selective blockade, permitting ships from non-combatant nations through a coastal corridor. At least 10 vessels have transited, and Japan may soon receive clearance. Iran could argue the strait is open while the US insists it is not—the definition of “fully open” is itself the negotiating space. British PM Starmer and Trump discussed the crisis Sunday evening, agreeing that reopening Hormuz is essential. NATO Secretary General Rutte expressed confidence the alliance could reopen the strait.
Iran Opens a Financial Warfare Front
In a significant escalation beyond the military domain, Iranian Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf warned on Sunday that financial entities purchasing US Treasurys—and thereby financing the US military budget—would be considered legitimate targets. While the operational capacity to act on this threat is unclear, the rhetoric itself introduces a new vector of risk. If regional sovereign wealth funds or central banks interpret this as a credible threat to their Treasury holdings or custodial arrangements, even a marginal shift in demand could pressure the long end of the curve. TLT fell 1.9% on Friday. The 10-year yield backdrop is already complicated by the Fed’s hawkish hold and energy-driven inflation expectations. A financial warfare dimension layered on top of the physical supply disruption would represent a qualitative escalation in how this conflict transmits to US asset prices.
Sunday’s Strikes — Tehran Hit Again, Regional Spread Continues
Israel launched new airstrikes on eastern Tehran on Sunday, extending the sustained campaign that has now hit at least 29 of Iran’s 31 provinces according to ACLED data. The war death toll across the region has surpassed 2,300. Domestically, Tehran residents described long queues for fuel, with authorities limiting purchases to 20 liters per car. The humanitarian toll is mounting: the UN reports 3 million Iranians displaced, with at least 18 hospitals struck according to the WHO. Saudi Arabia expelled Iran’s military attaché and four embassy staff over the weekend—a notable shift from Riyadh’s earlier neutral posture. The conflict now involves military operations across at least six countries. Each new front complicates any ceasefire framework and extends the timeline for Hormuz normalization.
