Markets — Week in Review

Fifth Straight Weekly Decline — Dow Enters Correction
The S&P 500 fell 2.1% on the week to 6,369, marking what CNBC reported as the fifth consecutive weekly decline—the longest losing streak since 2022. The Dow dropped 1.73% on Friday alone to 45,166, officially entering correction territory alongside the Nasdaq, which now sits approximately 13% below its October record. Friday’s session accelerated the damage: Brent crude surged 4.2% to $112.57, WTI briefly touched $100 for the first time since 2022, and the VIX surged to approximately 31, its highest sustained level in over a year. Only energy posted a meaningful gain for the week as the war premium continued to widen. The dividend sleeve outperformed again: DVY gained ground while IUSG shed nearly 3% on Thursday alone.

Section 02

Geopolitics

Trump Extends Strike Deadline to April 6 — Buying Time, Not Resolution
The most significant weekend development: Trump issued a second postponement of power-plant strikes, pushing the deadline from the original late-March window to April 6 at 8:00 PM ET. He cited productive conversations facilitated through Pakistani mediators, though Iran’s parliament speaker publicly denied any negotiations are taking place and characterized the claim as market manipulation. The gap between the two positions remains unchanged—Iran’s five-point counter-demands, including war reparations and full Hormuz sovereignty, are functionally incompatible with Washington’s requirements. The extension buys time for diplomacy but does not narrow the substantive distance between the parties. Meanwhile, according to Axios, the Pentagon has prepared contingency plans for an operation against Kharg Island, through which 90% of Iran’s oil exports historically flowed. Iran is reportedly fortifying the island with additional air defenses. Gulf allies are privately urging restraint. The April 6 deadline now aligns uncomfortably with the mid-April reserve depletion window identified in prior analysis.
Hormuz Is Functionally Closed — The Dual Blockade Has No Modern Precedent
Vessel tracking data cited by CNBC reveals the scale of the Hormuz disruption: only 142 ships transited the strait between March 1 and March 25, compared with 2,652 during the same period in 2025. On March 25, a single container ship and zero tankers passed through—versus a historical daily average of approximately 138 vessels. Iran has implemented what analysts are calling a toll-booth system, charging millions of dollars per transit; at least two vessels reportedly paid in yuan. Nearly 2,000 ships remain stranded near the strait. Compounding the disruption, Houthi attacks in the Red Sea resumed on February 28, meaning both Hormuz and the Suez alternative are simultaneously obstructed—a scenario the IEA has characterized as the largest supply disruption in the history of the oil market. Goldman Sachs estimates the geopolitical risk premium embedded in Brent at $14–$18 per barrel. For IOWN’s energy sleeve, the duration of this dual blockade directly supports the thesis for CVX, VLO, and OKE.
Troop Buildup Accelerates — Over 50,000 in Theater
US force posture continues to expand. The 82nd Airborne Division’s deployment of 2,000–3,000 paratroopers is underway, joining two Marine Expeditionary Units already en route. Approximately 50,000 US military personnel are already in the broader theater, with an additional 8,000 troops deploying. The human cost continues to mount: Iran’s Health Ministry reports approximately 1,900 killed since February 28, Lebanon’s Health Ministry reports 1,116 from related Israeli strikes, and 13 US service members have been killed with approximately 290 wounded. India is rekindling energy ties with Russia to hedge against Gulf supply disruption, according to Reuters. Europe is scaling back climate targets to ease the energy shock. The war’s ripple effects are reshaping alliances and energy policy across four continents. GD and LMT, IOWN’s defense holdings, have outperformed the broader market throughout the conflict as the fiscal beneficiaries of an extended engagement.
