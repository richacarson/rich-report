Section 02

Geopolitics

Houthis Enter the War — The Third Chokepoint Threat
Yemen’s Houthi movement fired ballistic missiles at southern Israel on Saturday, marking the group’s formal entry into the month-old conflict. The IDF reported intercepting the projectiles, and a second launch targeted Eilat. The immediate military impact appears limited — Israel’s air defenses have historically managed Houthi missile threats — but the strategic implications could be significant. The Houthis have not yet moved to close the Bab el-Mandeb Strait, the 16-mile-wide Red Sea chokepoint through which approximately 10% of global seaborne oil passes. This distinction matters because Saudi Arabia has been routing additional oil shipments through the Red Sea specifically to bypass the Hormuz closure. If the Houthis escalate from missile strikes on Israel to maritime interdiction — a capability they demonstrated extensively in 2024 — the last major alternative route for Gulf oil is eliminated. The resulting scenario, in which Hormuz, the Suez alternative, and Bab el-Mandeb are all simultaneously disrupted, would have no clear modern precedent. For IOWN, MATX is directly exposed to global shipping disruption, while CVX, VLO, and OKE could benefit from further supply tightening.
Day 29 — The Conflict Widens and Casualties Mount
The war’s toll continues to accelerate. Iran fired six ballistic missiles and 29 drones at Prince Sultan Air Base in Saudi Arabia on Friday, according to NPR, wounding at least 15 US service members — including at least five in serious condition — and reportedly destroying a US Air Force E-3 Sentry AWACS aircraft. Total US casualties since February 28 have reached 13 killed and over 300 wounded, according to CENTCOM. The IDF launched a widespread wave of strikes against Iranian government infrastructure in Tehran overnight Saturday into Sunday, according to CNN, while Israel struck the Bushehr nuclear plant for the third time in ten days. Air raid sirens were activated across Bahrain and Kuwait early Sunday morning. The IRGC has issued an ultimatum demanding the US condemn strikes on Iranian academic institutions by March 30, threatening to expand attacks to additional civilian infrastructure. The 31st Marine Expeditionary Unit and USS Tripoli arrived in theater over the weekend, with speculation mounting around a potential operation against Kharg Island. GD and LMT, IOWN’s defense holdings, continue to benefit from the fiscal trajectory of an extended and widening engagement.
The April 6 Deadline and the Narrowing Window for Diplomacy
Pakistan is hosting a diplomatic summit starting Monday, March 30, bringing together foreign ministers from Saudi Arabia, Turkey, and Egypt alongside special envoy Steve Witkoff. Iran’s position remains unchanged: Foreign Minister Araghchi maintains that no direct negotiations have occurred and none are planned. The gap between the two sides — Iran’s demands for reparations, Hormuz sovereignty, and a halt to assassinations versus Washington’s requirements for denuclearization and waterway reopening — shows no sign of narrowing, even as overnight strikes on Tehran suggest the conflict is intensifying rather than approaching resolution. The April 6 deadline, when Trump’s pause on energy infrastructure strikes expires, is eight days away. BCA Research estimates that lost global oil supply from the conflict could double by mid-April as strategic reserves are depleted, meaning the strike deadline and the reserve exhaustion window may converge within the same week. OPEC+ has confirmed no plans to increase output before Q3 2026. If April 6 passes without a framework and reserves run dry days later, there may be no policy lever remaining to contain the energy shock.
