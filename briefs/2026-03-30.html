Section 02

Geopolitics

Houthis Enter the War — The Third Chokepoint Is Now in Play
Yemen’s Houthi movement fired ballistic missiles at southern Israel on Saturday, marking the group’s formal entry into the month-old conflict. Israel’s military intercepted the projectiles. A Houthi spokesman indicated attacks would continue until military operations across all fronts cease. The immediate military impact appears limited, but the strategic implications could be significant. The Houthis have not yet moved to close the Bab el-Mandeb Strait, the 16-mile-wide Red Sea chokepoint through which approximately 10% of global seaborne oil passes. This distinction matters: Saudi Arabia has specifically increased Red Sea shipments to bypass the Hormuz closure. If the Houthis escalate from missile strikes to maritime interdiction—a capability they demonstrated extensively in 2024—the last major alternative route for Gulf oil is eliminated. The resulting scenario, in which Hormuz, the Suez alternative, and Bab el-Mandeb are simultaneously disrupted, would have no clear modern precedent. MATX is directly exposed to global shipping disruption. CVX, VLO, and OKE could benefit from further supply tightening.
Iran Strikes Gulf Aluminum Smelters — War Reaches Industrial Supply Chains
In a significant escalation beyond energy infrastructure, Iran struck Emirates Global Aluminium in Abu Dhabi and Aluminium Bahrain over the weekend, according to Bloomberg. EGA reported “significant damage” at its Al Taweelah site, while Alba is assessing facility damage. Iran’s Revolutionary Guards stated the strikes were retaliation for US-Israeli attacks on Iranian steel plants. LME aluminum surged approximately 6% to $3,492 per ton on Monday, its highest level in four years. LME warehouse stocks have dropped more than 60% since May. Analysts at Britannia Global Markets warned the strikes could push aluminum toward $4,000 per ton. For IOWN, the direct exposure is limited, but the second-order effects are meaningful: STLD (–1.98% Friday) and CAT (–1.11%) are both sensitive to industrial metals pricing dynamics, while the aluminum disruption adds another dimension to the inflation overhang that constrains the Fed.
Pakistan Hosts Talks — The Diplomatic Window Cracks Open Again
Pakistan hosted a two-day summit beginning Sunday, bringing together foreign ministers from Saudi Arabia, Turkey, and Egypt alongside US special envoy Witkoff. Pakistani Foreign Minister Dar announced that both the US and Iran have expressed confidence in Pakistan to facilitate “meaningful talks” in the coming days—which, if they occur, would represent the first direct or mediated negotiations since the war began on February 28. Trump amplified the signal, posting that the US is in “serious discussions with A NEW, AND MORE REASONABLE, REGIME.” The reference to a “new regime” is notable: since the strike that killed Supreme Leader Khamenei on February 28, the political authority structure in Tehran has been fragmented among military commanders, parliamentary leadership, and the surviving clerical establishment. Whether this fragmentation produces a negotiating partner or merely prolongs the absence of one is the fundamental uncertainty. Iran’s parliament speaker continues to deny any negotiations are occurring. The April 6 deadline, when the pause on energy infrastructure strikes expires, is seven days away. Meanwhile, Iran warned that US ground troops would be “set on fire,” and the Pentagon is reportedly preparing for weeks of limited ground operations according to the Washington Post.
