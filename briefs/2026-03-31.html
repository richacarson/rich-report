WTI Settles Above $100 for the First Time Since July 2022
WTI closed at $102.88 on Monday—its first settlement above triple digits since July 2022. Brent finished at $112.78 and is trading near $113 in early Tuesday trading. March is now the strongest month on record for both benchmarks: Brent gained approximately 55% and WTI approximately 53%, surpassing the Gulf War record set in 1990 per CNBC. The energy sleeve remains the portfolio’s primary source of positive returns. VLO, CVX, OKE, and CNX have been among the only consistently green names across the entire conflict. For IOWN, the global inflation impulse reinforces the case for dividend names with pricing power—CHD (+1.58% Friday), CL (+0.18%), and ADP—while growth names face compressing multiples from higher real discount rates globally.

Section 02

Geopolitics

Trump’s Dual Track — Kharg Island Threats Meet 15-Point Claims
Monday’s most consequential development was the simultaneous escalation and de-escalation of rhetoric from Washington. On Truth Social, Trump threatened to “completely obliterate” Iran’s electric plants, oil wells, and Kharg Island if the Strait of Hormuz is not immediately reopened. In a Financial Times interview, he said his “preference would be to take the oil,” comparing the operation to US action in Venezuela. Kharg Island handles 90% of Iran’s crude exports—approximately 1.5 million barrels per day. The Pentagon is reportedly preparing for limited ground operations, potentially including raids on Kharg and coastal sites near Hormuz, according to the Washington Post. Yet in the same news cycle, Trump claimed Iran has agreed to “most of” a 15-point US demand list conveyed through Pakistan. Iran’s Foreign Minister Araghchi dismissed the proposals as “unrealistic.” The gap between these two signals—military seizure threats and claimed diplomatic breakthroughs—is the source of the binary risk that defines every session this week.
Iran Strikes Kuwait Desalination Plant — The War Reaches Water
In a significant escalation beyond energy infrastructure, an Iranian attack struck a power and water desalination plant in Kuwait on Sunday evening, killing one worker and damaging the Sabiya facility, according to Al Jazeera. Kuwait relies on desalination for 90% of its drinking water, and the Gulf region produces 40% of the world’s desalinated water supply. Kuwait’s defense ministry reported 14 missiles and 12 drones detected in Kuwaiti airspace the same day, with several targeting a military camp and injuring 10 servicemen. The IRGC Navy commander responsible for Strait of Hormuz operations, Ali Reza Tangsiri, was confirmed dead by Iranian state media—a development that could affect Iran’s naval posture in the strait. Kuwait Petroleum Corporation’s CEO stated that Iran is effectively holding the world economy hostage. The war’s expansion from oil infrastructure to water and power in Gulf states introduces risks that extend well beyond energy markets.
Spain Closes Airspace — NATO’s Fracture Line Widens
Spain announced Monday that it has closed its airspace to all US military aircraft involved in the Iran war, extending an earlier restriction that barred the use of the Rota and Morón bases, according to Bloomberg. Defence Minister Robles confirmed the closure was communicated to US forces from the outset. Prime Minister Sánchez has described the war as illegal and reckless. Trump responded by calling Spain “terrible” and threatening trade cuts. Spain is the most vocal NATO dissenter, but the fracture line may widen: European parliaments face growing domestic pressure as the conflict’s economic costs mount. German inflation at 2.7% provides a tangible cost measure for voters. Meanwhile, Pakistan’s Foreign Minister Dar is heading to Beijing at China’s invitation, per Bloomberg, suggesting the diplomatic track is expanding beyond the four-nation framework. Whether Beijing’s involvement accelerates or complicates negotiations remains to be seen. The April 6 deadline is six days away.
