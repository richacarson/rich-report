Section 01

Markets

Two Days of Hope Meet Nineteen Minutes of Reality
Wednesday extended Tuesday’s relief rally as the S&P 500 closed at 6,575, up 0.72%, and the Nasdaq gained 1.16%. Industrials led (+1.67%) and tech continued its rebound (+1.51%), while energy fell 3.74% as ceasefire expectations drove a two-day crude oil decline. IUSG outperformed DVY by 1.6 percentage points—the second consecutive session of growth leading dividend value. Then, at 9 PM ET, President Trump addressed the nation on Operation Epic Fury. Rather than announcing the ceasefire framework that the prior two sessions had been pricing, he warned that the US would continue to strike Iran with extreme force over the next two to three weeks and threatened Iranian power infrastructure if no deal is reached. S&P 500 futures fell 1.29% overnight, Nasdaq futures dropped 1.59%, and Dow futures slid approximately 540 points. Brent crude surged 6.3% to approximately $108, erasing its two-day decline. The pattern the prior brief identified—that the rally was built on what Trump said, not on what Iran agreed to—has repeated.
The Overnight Damage Is Global
Asian markets sold off sharply after the speech. South Korea’s Kospi fell 5.5%, Japan’s Nikkei declined 1.4%, and India’s Nifty 50 opened down 1.38%, according to CNBC. The 10-year Treasury yield rose 5 basis points to 4.37%. Gold reversed Wednesday’s 1.75% gain, falling 2.5% to approximately $4,690 as the dollar index rose 0.5%. WTI crude jumped 6.4% to approximately $107. Bitcoin retreated from ~$68,500 to approximately $66,500 overnight. The VIX closed at 24.54 on Wednesday, down 3.4% during the session, but is positioned to gap significantly higher at Thursday’s open. Bank of America economists now project oil remaining at $100 per barrel through the remainder of 2026, which would sustain the inflationary pressure that has constrained the Fed for months.
ISM Prices at 78.3 — The Stagflation Report
March ISM Manufacturing registered 52.7—the third consecutive month of expansion and the highest reading since August 2022. But the headline masks the tension beneath. The Prices Index surged 7.8 points to 78.3, its highest level since June 2022, driven by steel and aluminum tariffs and petroleum costs from the Middle East conflict. The ISM noted that the Prices Index has risen 19.3 points in the last two months. Employment contracted for the 30th consecutive month at 48.7. The ISM report captures the stagflation dynamic in a single release: output expanding, costs accelerating, and manufacturers unable to hire. For IOWN, the pricing pressure flows directly through to the industrial sleeve—CAT (+3.09%), STLD (+1.58%), and PCAR (+1.86%) all benefited from Wednesday’s expansion reading, but margin compression from raw material costs remains the medium-term risk if oil sustains these levels.
Compressed Calendar — Payrolls Arrive While Markets Sleep
ADP reported 62,000 private sector jobs added in March, exceeding the 40,000 consensus, with education and health services (+58,000) the primary driver. But the report that matters—March nonfarm payrolls—arrives Friday at 8:30 AM ET while the NYSE is closed for Good Friday. The BLS has confirmed the April 3 release date. The Wall Street consensus is 59,000 following February’s 92,000 contraction. Whatever the number reveals, the market’s reaction will be entirely deferred to Monday morning—which is also April 6, the date Trump has signaled energy strikes could resume. Two catalysts converging on a single opening bell, with no opportunity to adjust positioning over the weekend.

Section 02

Geopolitics

Operation Epic Fury — Escalation Framed as Victory
In a 19-minute primetime address, Trump cast the Iran campaign as an extraordinary military achievement, describing the destruction of Iran’s navy, air force, and nuclear infrastructure, according to NPR. He stated that core strategic objectives were nearing completion. But the operative language pointed in a different direction: the US would continue to strike Iran with extreme force over the coming weeks, and if no deal is reached, power plants and civilian infrastructure could be targeted. Oil prices rose nearly 4% during the speech itself. Thirteen US service members have died and 348 have been wounded, according to Pentagon figures. Trump acknowledged that gasoline prices have risen more than 30% since the war began, per CNBC, but promised prices would decline rapidly once the conflict ends. The market read the speech as continuation, not conclusion.
Iran Denies Ceasefire Request — Diplomatic Window Narrows
Despite Trump’s morning claim on Truth Social that Iran’s president had requested a ceasefire, Foreign Minister Araghchi told Al Jazeera that Tehran is not seeking a ceasefire and that no negotiations are underway. A senior Iranian source told Reuters that Iran would accept only a guaranteed permanent end to the war, not a temporary cessation. The China-Pakistan five-point initiative from earlier this week remains on the table, but no face-to-face talks have been arranged. Pakistan’s foreign minister traveled to Beijing at China’s invitation, per Bloomberg, suggesting the multilateral framework continues to develop. But the gap between Washington and Tehran has widened rather than narrowed over the past 48 hours. The diplomatic track that appeared to be leading the military track on Tuesday has stalled.
Multiple Fronts Complicate Any Resolution
Israel continues operations against Hezbollah in Lebanon, where more than 1,300 people have been killed and over one million displaced since early March, according to CNN. The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed to most commercial shipping. The conflict’s expansion across multiple theaters—Iran proper, Lebanon, Gulf shipping lanes—makes a single ceasefire framework increasingly difficult to construct. For the portfolio, the multi-front dynamic suggests the energy premium is unlikely to unwind quickly even if a bilateral US-Iran arrangement materializes. IOWN’s energy names (CVX –4.59%, VLO –2.27%, OKE –3.35%) sold off Wednesday on ceasefire hopes that have since evaporated; those positions may regain ground at Thursday’s open as oil surges back.
