Markets — Week in Review

Three Reversals in 96 Hours
The shortened week delivered the conflict’s widest emotional range. Tuesday’s 2.91% S&P rally on Trump’s exit signal was the most powerful of the war. Wednesday extended the gain as the S&P added 0.72%. Then Trump’s Wednesday evening address—threatening extreme force for two to three more weeks—sent futures down sharply overnight. Thursday opened with the Dow down more than 600 points before Iranian state media reported that Tehran and Oman are drafting a protocol to monitor Strait of Hormuz transit. The report reversed the entire session: the S&P closed at 6,583, up 0.11% on the day and approximately 3.4% on the week, according to Bloomberg. The net result is that equity markets recovered the prior week’s losses, but the path required absorbing three distinct narrative reversals.

Gold’s Sharpest Decline of the Conflict Rewrites the Rate Narrative
Gold fell 2.7% on Thursday to $4,622, one of its sharpest single-session declines since the war began. The catalyst was twofold: the Senate Banking Committee officially scheduled Kevin Warsh’s Fed Chair confirmation hearing for the week of April 13, and early April CPI nowcasts surged to 3.71%, up from 3.25% in March, driven by the energy shock. The combination eliminated any remaining market expectation for rate cuts in 2026. Higher nominal yields increase the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding bullion, and the stronger dollar compounds the pressure. IOWN’s gold miners—GFI (+6.01% Wednesday) and AEM (+3.50%)—had been the portfolio’s strongest performers during the conflict; the Warsh hearing introduces a changed rate environment that could persist through confirmation. Rate-sensitive holdings across the portfolio (NEE, BKH, ATO) face similar headwinds in a zero-cut world.
The week proved that the market can rally and sell off on the same information depending on the hour. What persists through the noise is the portfolio’s construction—dividend cash flows that compound through the cycle, energy exposure that benefits from the disruption itself, and the discipline to distinguish between a headline and a thesis. Section 02

Geopolitics

The Iran-Oman Hormuz Protocol — The Week’s Most Significant Diplomatic Development
Iranian state media reported Thursday that Tehran and Oman are drafting a joint protocol to monitor transit through the Strait of Hormuz, according to IRNA. Deputy Foreign Minister Gharibabadi stated that vessels would coordinate with both coastal states to ensure safe passage, describing the framework as facilitating rather than restricting navigation. The announcement reversed Thursday’s 600-point Dow decline within hours. If implemented, the protocol could represent the first concrete pathway to partial Hormuz reopening that does not require a ceasefire or a bilateral US-Iran agreement. For IOWN’s energy holdings, the distinction matters: a negotiated monitoring regime may gradually reduce the shipping premium without the sudden unwinding that a ceasefire would produce, potentially allowing CVX, VLO, and OKE to adjust rather than absorb a one-day shock. Skeptics note, as Fortune reported, that Iran would effectively collect tolls on the waterway it blockaded—but markets appeared willing to accept that framework on Thursday.
UN Security Council Hormuz Vote Delayed — China Opposes Force Authorization
The UN Security Council postponed a scheduled Friday vote on a Bahrain-drafted resolution authorizing defensive force to protect Hormuz shipping, according to France 24 and Al-Monitor. The delay was attributed to the Good Friday holiday, but the political obstacles are substantive: China’s ambassador stated that authorizing member states to use force would inevitably lead to further escalation. The draft would greenlight voluntary multinational naval partnerships to use all defensive means necessary to secure transit passage for at least six months. If the resolution passes next week, it provides a legal framework for naval escorts that could complement the Iran-Oman monitoring protocol. If it is vetoed by China or Russia, the US may pursue unilateral escort operations that would represent a significant escalation within the Strait itself. The vote’s outcome adds another variable to Monday’s already crowded catalyst window.
Day 34 — The Gap Between Diplomacy and the Battlefield Continues to Widen
The same day Iran drafted the Hormuz monitoring protocol, US and Israeli strikes hit the B1 bridge between Tehran and Karaj, according to Gulf News, killing eight and wounding 95 in two separate attacks—the second striking as emergency teams responded to the first. Kuwait’s air defenses intercepted hostile missile and drone attacks, a reminder that the conflict’s spillover into neighboring Gulf states continues. Approximately 150 freight ships, including oil tankers, remain stalled near the Strait. The diplomatic track and the military track are producing contradictory signals on the same day—and for the portfolio, that gap is the source of the volatility. Every diplomatic signal lifts risk assets; every military development reverses the gain. The construction must hold through both.
