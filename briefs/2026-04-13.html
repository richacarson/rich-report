The Ceasefire Rally Reverses in 72 Hours — Blockade Begins Today at 10 AM ET
The most significant escalation since the conflict began has arrived. The US-Iran peace talks in Islamabad collapsed Sunday after 21 hours of face-to-face negotiations led by Vice President JD Vance, and Trump responded by ordering a US Navy blockade of the Strait of Hormuz effective today at 10 AM ET, according to CENTCOM. Oil surged on the news: Brent jumped approximately 7% overnight to near $102, with WTI climbing as much as 9.3% to above $105. S&P 500 futures are down approximately 0.7%, Dow futures down roughly 400 points, Nasdaq futures off 0.6%. Friday’s session was essentially flat ahead of CPI and the weekend talks: S&P –0.07%, Dow –0.55%, Nasdaq +0.14%. UVXY rose 1.92% Friday and is positioned to gap higher at today’s open. The rally that carried the S&P 7% higher over the prior eight sessions is now at risk of a rapid unwind as the blockade introduces a new risk dimension: direct US military enforcement in the Strait rather than reliance on Iran’s selective access framework.
March CPI Confirmed 3.3% YoY — Energy Drove the Headline, Core Remained Contained
Friday’s March CPI report showed headline inflation rising 0.9% month-over-month, lifting the annual rate to 3.3%—the highest level since May 2024 and up from 2.4% in February, according to the BLS. Energy rose 10.9% for the month, with gasoline prices surging 21.2%, accounting for nearly three-quarters of the total headline increase. Core CPI, excluding food and energy, rose just 0.2% month-over-month and 2.6% year-over-year—both 0.1 percentage point below consensus forecasts. The split tells the complete macro story: the war’s energy shock is flowing through to headline prices, but the underlying inflation process remains contained. For the Fed, this is the most difficult possible print: a headline number that forces continued restraint and a core reading that would, in any other environment, support easing. The zero-cut posture for 2026 remains intact. With the blockade now threatening to push energy prices higher again, April CPI risks accelerating further.

The semiconductor recovery trade has been the dominant non-energy theme: LRCX has gained for several consecutive sessions, TSMC topped Q1 sales on AI demand, and AMD continues its momentum on CPU pricing power and AI infrastructure deals. That thesis does not depend on the ceasefire holding.

Section 02

Geopolitics

Islamabad Peace Talks Collapse After 21 Hours — Gaps Too Wide to Bridge
The US delegation, led by Vice President Vance, met with Iranian and Pakistani negotiators for more than 21 hours over the weekend, but the talks ended without an agreement. The US described the failure as stemming from Iran’s unwillingness to give up its efforts to obtain a nuclear weapon, according to NPR. Iran’s demands, per Iranian state TV, included sovereign control over the Strait of Hormuz, payment of war reparations, and a ceasefire across the broader region including Lebanon. These demands were effectively the same five-point counter-proposal Iran first articulated in late March, which has proven incompatible with Washington’s nuclear and navigational requirements. The collapse confirms the analysis from prior briefs: the two positions were not negotiating distance—they were fundamental disagreements about the terms of engagement. The ceasefire announced April 8 is now effectively over, though Trump’s CNN statement that the ceasefire is “holding well” even as the blockade begins creates a rhetorical contradiction that markets are struggling to price.
The US Navy Blockade — A New Phase of the Conflict
Trump announced via Truth Social that the US Navy will block any ship attempting to enter or leave the Strait of Hormuz, with CENTCOM confirming a 10 AM ET Monday start time. The order states that vessels transiting to and from non-Iranian ports will not be impeded—an important qualification that distinguishes this from a total closure. The blockade itself is an act of war under international law, representing a material shift from indirect support of Israeli operations to direct US military enforcement. Analysts cited by CNN characterize the operation as a major military endeavor requiring sustained naval presence, escorts, and rules of engagement that could trigger direct US-Iran confrontation in the waterway. Iran’s response called the move “piracy,” according to the Times of Israel, and the country has vowed to target any vessel participating in the blockade. The duration remains unspecified—Trump stated the blockade would remain in effect until Iran opens the Strait to all traffic, a demand Tehran has consistently rejected.
Allies Fracture — UK, China Refuse to Support
UK Prime Minister Starmer stated on BBC radio that Britain will not join the US blockade. China urged restraint and called for renewed talks, according to Reuters. Turkey expressed concern about any new Iran-US rules for the Strait. The fracture is significant: the blockade requires sustained multinational naval cooperation to be effective, and the UK’s refusal signals that the broader coalition supporting US operations in the region has narrowed. India has not publicly committed. The 22-nation coalition that issued a joint statement condemning Iran’s blockade in late March has not reassembled for the US operation. For IOWN, the allied fracture introduces a new variable: the blockade may be sustained longer and at greater cost than the market is currently pricing, and its enforcement may be more limited than the announcement implies. Defense holdings (GD, LMT) benefit from sustained US military deployment but face complexity if the operation becomes politically controversial domestically.
