Markets

The Blockade Rally — Equities Absorb the Escalation and Push Higher
Monday’s session produced a counterintuitive rally in the face of material escalation. The S&P 500 rose 0.98%, the Dow gained 0.60%, the Nasdaq added 1.03%, and the Russell 2000 led at +1.44%. UVXY collapsed 5.61% as volatility compressed despite the active US Navy blockade of Iranian ports that began at 10 AM ET Monday. Tech (+2.10%) and financials (+1.75%) led sectors; staples (–1.00%) and utilities (–1.21%) were the only meaningful laggards. Reports that the US and Iran are considering further negotiations ahead of the two-week ceasefire window expiration appear to have steadied risk sentiment even as the physical blockade remained in effect. Oil is retreating in early Tuesday trading, with Brent near $98 and WTI near $96.57—a reversal from Monday’s 7% blockade-driven surge. S&P futures are little changed in the premarket as the market awaits March PPI at 8:30 AM ET and the start of Q1 bank earnings season.

Section 02

Geopolitics

The Blockade Is Active — But the Diplomatic Track Has Not Closed
The US Navy blockade of Iranian ports commenced Monday at 10 AM ET and remains in force. CENTCOM confirmed the operation targets only vessels originating from or destined for Iranian ports; transit through the Strait for non-Iranian commerce is unimpeded. Iran denounced the action as piracy and staged protests in Tehran, while the UK’s refusal to participate and China’s call for restraint have left the US operation largely unilateral among Western powers. However, reports cited by Al Jazeera indicate that Trump stated Tehran wants a deal, and Pakistan has offered to host a second round of talks in Islamabad. The market interpretation: the blockade is a pressure tactic within an ongoing negotiation, not a permanent rupture. If that reading holds, the current two-week ceasefire window (announced April 8) could be extended via fresh negotiations before the underlying hostilities resume. If it proves incorrect, the blockade evolves into a sustained military operation with open-ended supply chain consequences.
Oil Retreats on Negotiation Signals — Brent Gives Back the Blockade Premium
Brent crude fell approximately 1% to $98 in early Tuesday trading, with WTI down to $96.57. The reversal unwinds a portion of Monday’s 7% blockade-driven surge and reflects market repricing around the renewed negotiation reports. Analysts cited by CNBC note that the blockade’s practical impact may be limited given that Hormuz transit had already been substantially disrupted since Iran’s selective-access regime was imposed in March; the blockade formalizes the disruption rather than materially extending it. For the IOWN energy sleeve, this reprices the war-premium trade: CVX (+1.71% Monday), VLO (+1.36%), and OKE (–1.02%) are transitioning from a war-premium-expansion trade to a structural-disruption-persistence trade. The thesis remains intact given that Hormuz reopening to pre-war levels would still require extensive reconstruction and verification.
