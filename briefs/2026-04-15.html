Markets

Breakout Confirmed — Index Within Striking Distance of All-Time High
Tuesday’s session delivered a clean breakout. The S&P 500 rose 1.22%, the Nasdaq surged 1.82%, the Dow gained 0.70%, and the Russell 2000 added 1.38%. UVXY fell 2.05% as volatility compression continued. Discretionary (+2.21%), tech (+1.60%), and communications services (+1.52%) led sectors. Energy was the clear laggard at –2.03% as crude gave back prior-session gains. The index is now within approximately seven points of the January 28 all-time high at 7,002.28, with S&P futures hovering near 7,005 in the premarket per TheStreet. The confluence of catalysts was unusually favorable: a soft PPI print, strong Citigroup earnings, resumed US-Iran negotiation signals, and oil retreating 3–4% all combined to drive the advance. The question today is whether the tape can clear the January high on the open or whether another session of consolidation is required.
Carson’s Technical Framework — Major Resistance Zone Cleared, All Resistance Now in the Rearview
Tuesday’s rally took SPY through Carson’s major resistance zone at $691.90-$693.20 on a single session. The intraday print of $694.58 cleared both bounds, and the close at $694.46 settled above the zone. Both levels are marked hit on the technical register. Carson’s working view had anticipated a meaningful concentration of sell-side order flow at this band but allowed for a sustained breakout; the breakout has materialized. With all near-term resistance now cleared, the upside objective on the framework shifts toward the January all-time high of approximately 7,002 on the S&P 500 index (equivalent to roughly $700 on SPY). On the downside, the published support at $645.80 remains untouched and intact as the retracement anchor should the breakout reverse.

PPI +0.5% vs 1.1% Consensus — The Stagflation Pressure Has Softened
Tuesday’s March PPI release was the single most important macro data point of the month. Headline PPI rose just 0.5% month-over-month against the 1.1% consensus estimate, with annual pricing up 4.0%—the largest 12-month advance since February 2023 but well below feared levels, according to the BLS. Core PPI, excluding food and energy, rose only 0.1% versus the 0.5% estimate. Energy drove the headline: gasoline prices surged 15.7%, diesel jumped 42%, and jet fuel rose 30.7%, collectively accounting for roughly half the gain. Services prices were flat. The message is clear: the war’s energy shock is pushing through to headline inflation but has not broadened into core pricing pressure. For the Fed, this is the most constructive possible combination—hot headline that justifies the current hold posture, soft core that preserves the option for eventual easing. The zero-cut baseline for 2026 remains intact but the probability of further tightening has meaningfully declined.
Q1 Bank Earnings — Citi Leads, JPM Beats but NII Guidance Weighs
The bank reporting cycle opened with mixed but broadly constructive results. JPMorgan posted adjusted EPS of $5.94 versus the $5.46 consensus, driven by record quarterly trading revenue of $11.6 billion (+20% year-over-year), but the bank trimmed 2026 net interest income guidance to $103 billion from $104.5 billion, and shares declined approximately 3%. Citigroup significantly outpaced estimates with services revenue of $6.1 billion (+17% year-over-year), banking revenue up 15%, and equity underwriting fees surging 64%, establishing the bank as the quarter’s top performer among money-centers. Wells Fargo delivered in-line results ($1.60 vs $1.60 consensus) but softened its NII guidance, which shifted its valuation narrative. JNJ reported $2.70 versus the $2.69 estimate, an essentially in-line print. The bank tape reflects the broader macro read: operating businesses are strong, but the path of net interest margins remains constrained by the flat yield curve and the Fed’s indefinite hold.

Section 02

Geopolitics

Second Round of Talks — Both Sides Proposing Enrichment Suspension
The US and Iran are arranging a second round of ceasefire talks ahead of the April 21 expiration of the existing framework, according to Bloomberg. Both sides have now proposed a suspension of Iranian uranium enrichment, with the sticking point reduced to the timeframe rather than the concept. The US continues to demand the dismantling of Iran’s major enrichment facilities and the immediate reopening of the Strait of Hormuz; Tehran considers the ongoing US naval blockade a violation of the ceasefire. The narrowing of substantive gaps is the most constructive diplomatic development since the original 15-point plan was rejected in late March. If a framework extension materializes before April 21, oil could continue its retreat and the equity rally likely extends; if the window expires without an extension, the energy and defense premiums reassert.
The Blockade Is Fully Implemented — Oil Continues to Retreat
CENTCOM confirmed the naval blockade is fully implemented and has completely halted economic trade through Iranian ports, according to Al Jazeera. Few ships have transited the Strait since the blockade began on April 13. However, the operation’s practical supply impact appears limited given that Hormuz transit had already been substantially disrupted under Iran’s selective-access regime established in March. Oil has responded: Brent fell approximately 3% on Tuesday to $95 and WTI dropped 3.60%, reflecting market pricing of the renewed negotiation track rather than the blockade’s enforcement mechanics. The supply risk premium continues to compress, with Brent now meaningfully below the $115 highs recorded in late March.
