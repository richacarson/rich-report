Markets

S&P 500 Closes Above 7,000 for the First Time — War Losses Fully Erased
Wednesday completed what prior briefs described as a recovery that began at the March 30 low. The S&P 500 closed at 7,022.95, surpassing the previous January 28 intraday record of 7,002.28 and marking the first-ever close above 7,000. SPY closed at $699.94 (+0.79%) with an intraday high of $700.28. The Nasdaq Composite also reached an all-time high above 24,000, gaining 1.40% on the session. The Dow lagged at –0.16%. Tech (+1.60%) and discretionary (+1.49%) led sectors; industrials (–1.25%), materials (–1.21%), and utilities (–0.97%) were the notable laggards. The advance has erased the entirety of the war-era drawdown from its late-February onset through the March 30 low—a ten-session, approximately 10% recovery driven by the ceasefire, cooling inflation data, and the strongest bank earnings season in years. Futures are up approximately 0.19% in Thursday premarket. IUSG (+1.23%) continued to lead DVY (–0.22%)—growth over dividend value for the seventh session of the recovery.
Morgan Stanley Posts Record Revenue — Bank Earnings Season Delivers
The bank reporting cycle closed its second day with the strongest results in years. Morgan Stanley reported record quarterly revenue of $20.6 billion and EPS of $3.43 versus the $3.02 consensus, with institutional securities revenue at $10.7 billion, equity revenues at $5.1 billion (a segment record), and advisory revenue up 74% year-over-year. Bank of America beat consensus with $1.11 EPS versus $1.01, driven by equities trading revenue jumping 30% to $2.83 billion—its best trading quarter in fifteen years, per CNBC. BAC raised full-year NII guidance to 6–8% growth, a notable contrast to JPMorgan’s NII cut the prior day. The aggregate read from the bank cycle: trading and banking fees surged on the war-era volatility, credit quality remains resilient, and the rate environment is supporting margins through the curve structure rather than the level. For IOWN, SYF (+2.39% Tuesday) benefits from the same consumer credit resilience visible across the money-center results.
HOOD +10.41% — SEC Eliminates the $25,000 Day Trading Rule
Robinhood surged 10.41% to $87.32—its second consecutive session with a double-digit gain. The catalyst: the SEC eliminated the $25,000 pattern day trader rule, which had required margin account holders making four or more day trades within five business days to maintain at least $25,000 in equity. The new framework allows day traders to hold only sufficient equity to cover their trade exposure. For Robinhood, whose average account balance is well below traditional brokerage thresholds, the regulatory change could meaningfully expand daily trading activity and revenue. COIN rallied 6.23% to $195.90 and ETHA gained 2.74%. The digital sleeve has fully recovered from the March Clarity Act selloff and is now posting multi-week highs across most names. HUT, however, was roughly flat as gold miners gave back gains—GFI fell 5.45% and AEM declined 2.52%—reflecting the rotation out of precious metals and into growth and crypto.
Energy Continues to Fade as Oil Settles Into a Lower Range
Energy (–0.34%) was one of only four sectors in the red. VLO eased 0.25%, CVX declined 1.13%, and CNX gained 2.55% as an outlier. Oil continued its multi-session retreat: WTI dropped 1.02% and Brent eased 0.40%, with Brent now well below the $115 highs of late March. Iran’s announcement that it has halted petrochemical exports until further notice, reported by Reuters, may be intended as leverage ahead of any second round of negotiations. However, with markets now pricing a gradual de-escalation and the ceasefire framework holding despite mutual accusations of violations, the energy premium continues to compress. For IOWN’s energy sleeve, the transition from a war-premium trade to a structural-supply-deficit trade is complete; the names remain sound for the medium term but are no longer providing the short-term portfolio ballast they delivered during the active conflict phase.

Section 02

Geopolitics

Ceasefire Holds — Gaps Narrow but No Dates for Second Round
A senior Iranian official told Reuters on Wednesday that Iran and the US have narrowed differences following Pakistani mediation, though splits remain on key issues including the timeframe for uranium enrichment suspension and the status of the naval blockade. Pakistan’s government stated that no dates have been set for a second round of face-to-face talks, tempering expectations for an imminent framework extension. The ceasefire, set to expire around April 21, continues to hold despite mutual accusations of violations. For markets, the ongoing de-escalation—even absent formal progress—is sufficient to support the current risk-on posture. The absence of active military escalation has allowed the oil premium to compress, equities to recover, and the volatility regime to normalize. The risk remains that a collapse of the ceasefire before April 21 would rapidly reverse these dynamics.
Iran Halts Petrochemical Exports — Leverage or Exhaustion?
Iran announced a halt to all petrochemical exports until further notice, according to Reuters. The move could represent either a negotiating tactic ahead of potential second-round talks or a reflection of the operational damage sustained during the conflict—Israel’s strike on Asaluyeh, which accounted for a significant share of Iran’s petrochemical capacity, may have made the halt involuntary rather than strategic. Either interpretation supports the structural-supply-deficit thesis: even in a post-ceasefire environment, Iran’s ability to export petrochemical products is materially diminished, which has downstream implications for global chemical pricing and the cost structures of IOWN’s industrial holdings.
