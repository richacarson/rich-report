AMD +7.80% — 12-Day Winning Streak, the Longest in Two Decades
AMD surged 7.80% to $278.26 on Thursday, extending its winning streak to 12 consecutive sessions—the longest in over twenty years, according to Invezz. The catalyst was twofold: TSMC’s Q1 results confirmed that AI-driven chip demand remains robust, and Bernstein raised AMD’s price target to $265 from $235, citing the company’s expanding footprint in higher-margin server and data center chips through its Meta partnership. AMD has gained approximately 42% from its late-March low during the war’s most intense phase. The stock represents IOWN’s thesis at work: physical AI infrastructure demand that persists through geopolitical disruption. MARA also surged 10.32%, and FTNT recovered 3.47% from the zero-day vulnerability selloff. On the other side, TSM gave back 3.13% on profit-taking despite the strong Q1, and LRCX slipped 1.58%.
ABT –6.00% — Narrow Earnings Miss, Exact Sciences Acquisition Clouds the Read
Abbott Laboratories, reporting its Q1 results on Thursday, posted adjusted EPS of $1.15 versus the $1.16 consensus—a one-penny miss. Revenue of $11.2 billion exceeded expectations by approximately $160 million, representing 8% year-over-year growth. The Exact Sciences acquisition, which closed March 23, contributed approximately $3 billion in incremental annualized sales, with Cancer Diagnostics including Cologuard growing at mid-teens rates. Despite the revenue beat, the market sold ABT 6.00% to $95.47, making it IOWN’s second-worst performer on the day. The reaction suggests investors are skeptical of the Exact Sciences integration timeline or concerned about margin dilution. For the dividend sleeve, ABT remains a core cash-generative holding; the Q1 miss was narrow and the revenue trajectory is intact, but near-term price action may require patience.
Oil Rebounds as the Strait Remains Effectively Closed
Brent crude rose 3.72% and WTI gained 2.65% on Thursday, partially reversing the multi-session retreat. The catalyst: despite the ceasefire framework and renewed negotiations, the Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed, with Iran limiting transit and charging tolls exceeding $1 million per ship. The structural supply deficit has not been resolved by the ceasefire; it has merely been paused diplomatically. IOWN’s energy sleeve responded: VLO gained 2.87% and CNX added 1.04%. The energy trade is no longer providing the one-directional ballast of the war’s early weeks, but it retains relevance as a structural hedge against Hormuz normalization delays. Gold was flat (–0.09%) and is set for its fourth weekly gain, according to Reuters.

Section 02

Geopolitics

40-Nation Hormuz Summit Convenes Today — Without the US or Israel
French President Macron and UK Prime Minister Starmer are co-chairing a virtual summit today of approximately 40 nations focused on how to ensure security of commercial shipping through the Strait of Hormuz once the Iran war ends, according to NBC News and CBS News. The US and Israel are notably absent from the summit’s participant list, reflecting the diplomatic fracture that emerged when the UK declined to join the US Navy blockade. The objective is to create a “defensive multilateral mission” framework for Hormuz security that operates independently of the US blockade. If the summit produces a credible multinational naval framework, it could accelerate Hormuz normalization by providing Iran with an alternative security architecture to the bilateral US demands. For energy markets, a multilateral approach may be more durable than a US-only blockade and could reduce the geopolitical risk premium embedded in oil prices over time.
Israel-Lebanon Ceasefire Reached — One Front Closes as Hormuz Remains Gridlocked
Israel and Lebanon reached a ceasefire agreement, according to CBS News and NBC News, closing one of the multiple fronts that had complicated any comprehensive peace framework. The Lebanon front, where more than 1,300 people were killed since Israeli attacks escalated in early March, had been a persistent obstacle to broader negotiations because Iran linked the Lebanon operations to any ceasefire terms. With this front resolved, the remaining issues narrow to the nuclear question, Hormuz transit rights, and reparations—still formidable but incrementally more manageable. Trump stated the US is “very close” to a deal with Iran, and the ceasefire’s April 21 expiration remains four days away. If the framework extends, the equity market likely consolidates at these levels; if it lapses, the first real test of the new all-time highs arrives.
