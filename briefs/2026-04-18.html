From 7,000 to Hormuz — A Week That Reshaped the Post-Conflict Trade
The S&P 500 closed Friday at 7,126, posting its fourth consecutive all-time high after crossing 7,000 for the first time earlier in the week. Friday’s session delivered the defining development: Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi declared the Strait of Hormuz open to all commercial shipping, sending Brent crude down approximately 10% and equities to fresh records. The Nasdaq Composite extended its winning streak to 13 consecutive sessions, the longest since 1992. What began as a recovery from the March 30 conflict low has become something more: the market is now pricing a post-war equilibrium in which the energy risk premium compresses sharply and growth leadership reasserts itself.

Consumer Sentiment at a Record Low — But the Data May Already Be Stale
The University of Michigan’s Consumer Sentiment Index fell to 47.6 in April’s preliminary reading, the lowest in the survey’s 74-year history, with one-year inflation expectations surging from 3.8% to 4.8%. However, 98% of respondents were interviewed before the April 8 ceasefire announcement, meaning the data captures peak war anxiety and could partially recover in the final reading. The divergence between institutional behavior and consumer despair is notable: hedge funds deployed substantial capital into equities this week while consumers reported their worst outlook on record. Kevin Warsh’s Fed Chair confirmation hearing on Tuesday could become the next catalyst for rate expectations, with his financial disclosures revealing assets exceeding $135 million and the hearing facing a potential obstacle from Senator Tillis’s standoff with the White House over a DOJ investigation into Chair Powell.
Four all-time highs in a single week. The Strait declared open. Oil in retreat. Consumer sentiment at a 74-year low. The market is pricing a post-conflict world while consumers are still processing the conflict itself. Section 02

Geopolitics

Iran Declares Hormuz Open — But the US Blockade Remains
Friday produced the conflict’s most consequential diplomatic development since the April 8 ceasefire. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi announced that the Strait of Hormuz is open to all commercial shipping for the duration of the Lebanon ceasefire, according to NBC News and CNBC. The announcement sent Brent crude down approximately 10% on the session. However, the picture remains fragmented: President Trump stated that the US naval blockade of Iranian ports would remain in full force until a comprehensive peace deal is reached. The contradiction creates an uncertain environment for commercial shipping and energy markets. If vessels begin transiting the Strait in volume, the war premium embedded in oil prices could continue to compress. If the US blockade continues to deter traffic despite Iran’s declaration, the premium persists in a different form — one driven by enforcement risk rather than physical obstruction. For IOWN’s energy sleeve, this represents the clearest signal yet that the acute phase of the energy trade is winding down, though the structural supply deficit from damaged infrastructure and Iran’s halted petrochemical exports remains a medium-term tailwind for CVX, VLO, and OKE.
Paris Summit — 49 Nations Chart an Independent Course on Hormuz
French President Macron and UK Prime Minister Starmer co-chaired an emergency summit at the Élysée Palace on Friday, attended in person by German Chancellor Merz and Italian Prime Minister Meloni, with 45 additional countries joining by videoconference, according to Bloomberg and Al Jazeera. The summit called for the unconditional, unrestricted, and immediate reopening of the Strait and proposed a Franco-British-led defensive multinational naval mission to protect merchant vessels and conduct mine clearance operations once a sustainable ceasefire is achieved. A follow-up military planning summit is scheduled at the UK’s Permanent Joint Headquarters in Northwood next week. The absence of the US and Israel from the summit represents a notable moment of European strategic autonomy on energy security. If this multilateral framework produces a credible alternative to the US-led blockade, it could offer Iran a pathway to sustained Hormuz normalization independent of bilateral US terms — a development that would be structurally deflationary for global energy prices and constructive for the broader equity market.
Ceasefire Expires April 22 — Three Trading Days
The two-week US-Iran ceasefire, mediated by Pakistan and agreed on April 8, expires on Wednesday April 22. The White House has not formally requested an extension, though press secretary Leavitt indicated that conversations remain productive and ongoing. President Trump’s statement that the US may need to resume military operations if no deal materializes heightens the stakes. The Israel-Lebanon ceasefire, reached earlier this week, removed one front from the equation, but the nuclear question, Hormuz transit terms, and reparations remain unresolved. If the ceasefire extends, equity markets could consolidate at record levels with reduced tail risk. If it lapses without a framework, the recovery faces its first significant test at all-time highs. Carson’s SPY $645.80 retracement target remains the active downside level, sitting approximately 9% below Friday’s close — a threshold that would only come into play under a material re-escalation scenario.
