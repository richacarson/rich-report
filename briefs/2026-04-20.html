Markets

Friday’s Peace Rally Was the Strongest of the Recovery — Then the Weekend Happened
Friday delivered a session that encapsulated the market’s view of the post-conflict trajectory. The S&P 500 surged 1.21% to a new all-time high of approximately 7,101, the Dow gained 1.77%, the Nasdaq added 1.31%, and the Russell 2000 led at +2.16%. UVXY fell 1.80%. The catalyst was unmistakable: oil collapsed. WTI plunged 7.79% and Brent dropped 7.36% after Iran reportedly opened the Strait of Hormuz to all traffic and reports emerged that the US may release $20 billion in frozen Iranian funds in exchange for enriched uranium stockpiles. The first very large crude carrier cleared Hormuz on April 14 for the first time since the March 2 blockade. Discretionary (+2.36%), industrials (+1.87%), and tech (+1.53%) led sectors. Energy (–2.76%) was the sole meaningful laggard as the war premium unwound at pace. Fear & Greed improved to 29—the first reading outside Extreme Fear since the conflict began February 28. Then the weekend reversed the narrative.
Weekend Escalation — Ship Seizure, Hormuz Closure, and Futures Down 0.4%
The US Navy seized an Iranian cargo ship over the weekend, with Trump stating they blew a hole in its engine room. Iran responded by firing at vessels and abruptly halting all traffic in the Strait of Hormuz on Saturday, abandoning its earlier commitment to allow passage, according to CNBC. The ceasefire, scheduled to expire tomorrow (April 21), is now on the brink. Mediators have announced an in-principle agreement to extend it beyond April 22, but the White House has not formally agreed, and Iran’s Foreign Ministry denied that a second round of negotiations is planned. Trump stated on Sunday that American and Iranian negotiators would resume talks in Islamabad today, though Iran’s spokesperson contradicted this. S&P 500 futures are down approximately 0.4% in Monday premarket. European shares slid on the same concerns. Oil is likely rebounding from Friday’s 7%+ crash as the Hormuz closure reverses the de-escalation trade.
Oil’s 7% Friday Crash Was the Sharpest Peace-Driven Decline of the Conflict
WTI fell nearly 12% intraday on Friday to $83.85 before settling down 7.79%. Brent lost 7.36%. CNBC reported WTI plunged below $84 after Iran declared Hormuz open. The energy sleeve absorbed the full impact: VLO fell 7.48%, CVX dropped 2.21%, OKE declined 2.00%, and CNX shed 2.74%. The structural question now is whether Friday’s crash represented the beginning of a sustained unwind of the war premium or a temporary gap that the weekend’s escalation will fill. If Hormuz re-closes fully—which Saturday’s events suggest is possible—oil could retrace back toward the $95–$100 range that prevailed before Friday’s session. The energy sleeve’s role in the portfolio transitions daily between ballast and drag depending on the headline cycle; the underlying thesis of structural supply disruption remains valid until Hormuz transit normalizes to pre-war levels.
Growth, Gold, and Crypto All Rallied on Friday — The Breadth Was Exceptional

The question for today is whether the weekend’s ship seizure reverses this breadth or whether the market treats it as a temporary disruption in what it increasingly views as a de-escalation trajectory.

Section 02

Geopolitics

The Ceasefire Is at the Brink — Extension Claimed but Unconfirmed
The two-week ceasefire, mediated by Pakistan and announced April 8, is set to expire tomorrow, April 21. Mediators have announced an in-principle agreement to extend it beyond April 22, aiming to facilitate negotiations on Hormuz reopening, Iran’s 10-point proposal, and the linked Lebanon ceasefire. However, the White House told reporters that the US has not formally agreed to an extension, and Iran’s Foreign Ministry has denied reports of an extension, according to CNBC. The disconnect between mediators’ claims and the principals’ denials mirrors the pattern from the original Islamabad talks, where both sides denied negotiations even as they were underway. For markets, the ambiguity is the point: the ceasefire may be extended de facto even if neither side acknowledges it formally, preserving the diplomatic space without the political cost of a public concession.
Ship Seizure and Hormuz Reversal — The Weekend That Tested the Peace Thesis
The US Navy’s seizure of an Iranian cargo ship represented the most provocative direct US-Iran military interaction since the blockade began. Trump described the operation in characteristically direct terms. Iran’s response was immediate: on Saturday, it fired at vessels in the Strait and halted all traffic, reversing the partial opening that had driven Friday’s oil crash. The reversal is significant because it demonstrates that Iran retains the operational capability and willingness to close Hormuz at will, regardless of the ceasefire framework. The UK-France Hormuz summit of 40 nations on April 17 had been designed to create a multilateral security framework for the waterway; the weekend’s events suggest that framework has not yet deterred unilateral Iranian action. China voiced concern over the ship seizure and urged further talks, per Reuters.
