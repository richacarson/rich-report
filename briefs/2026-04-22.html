Markets

The Selloff Was Defensive-Led — Not What a Ceasefire Day Typically Looks Like
Tuesday’s tape inverted the conventional geopolitical playbook. The S&P 500 slipped 0.65% to roughly 7,041, the Dow fell 0.60%, the Nasdaq declined 0.38%, and the Russell 2000 lagged at –1.02%. UVXY rose 2.71%. The leadership was counter-intuitive: Real Estate (–1.93%), Utilities (–1.75%), Industrials (–1.41%), and Communication Services (–1.34%) led declines, while Energy (+1.45%) and Tech (+0.08%) held up. TLT fell 0.55% and the 20-year trade remains under pressure—the bond-proxy sleeves sold off as rate-cut odds for April remained near zero following resilient March inflation and payroll data. The move reads as positioning into higher-for-longer rates rather than a risk-off geopolitical hedge. S&P 500 futures are modestly higher overnight on AI optimism and the ceasefire extension, per Reuters.
DGX and SYF Validated the Dividend-Sleeve Thesis — GPC Held Despite a Narrow Miss
The first IOWN financial and healthcare prints of the cycle delivered. DGX surged 4.45% to $205.04 after reporting Q1 EPS of $2.50 vs. $2.37 consensus and revenue of $2.90 billion (up 9.2% year-over-year), then raising full-year 2026 EPS guidance to $10.63–$10.83 and revenue to $11.78–$11.90 billion. Advanced Diagnostics and Alzheimer’s blood-test volumes drove the beat. SYF delivered EPS of $2.27 vs. $2.22 est but closed down 1.21% as the market focused on forward credit-loss trajectory rather than the headline print. GPC reported EPS of $1.77 vs. $1.78 est (a narrow miss) and still gained 2.14% on constructive commentary around inflationary pricing recovery. The through-line: consumer-credit resilience and healthcare volume growth remain intact, which is exactly the read the dividend sleeve was built to provide.
Gold and Silver Were the Session’s Cleanest Casualty — The Ceasefire-Extension Trade Landed Here
GLD fell 2.83% and SLV collapsed 5.07% on Tuesday as the dollar firmed and news broke that Trump would extend the ceasefire pending Iran’s proposal, per Reuters and Axios. The miner sleeve absorbed the full impact: AEM dropped 6.15% and GFI fell 6.58%. Benzinga attributed the precious-metals weakness to Fed-chair-nominee Warsh’s hawkish Senate testimony and softening war-premium narratives. The miner selloff contrasts with WTI’s 5.71% session rally (USO) — oil rose on ceasefire uncertainty during the cash session before retreating overnight as the extension was confirmed. The divergence highlights that commodity markets are pricing different pieces of the same event: oil is pricing Hormuz operational risk, while gold is pricing dollar strength and the fading war premium. For IOWN’s growth sleeve, the miner drawdown is meaningful — AEM and GFI were among the conflict’s best performers and have now given back a substantial portion of the rally.
Semiconductor Leadership Held — AMD, NXPI, and KEYS Outperformed Into LRCX’s Print Today
AMD gained 3.47% to $284.49, NXPI added 1.43%, and KEYS rose 1.24% as the semis continued their multi-week recovery despite the broader tape. TSM closed +0.50%. Stifel and Morgan Stanley reset targets ahead of the earnings cycle, per Yahoo. The Amazon-Anthropic $25 billion investment announcement reinforced the AI capex narrative that is powering the group. LRCX (–1.82% to $258.37) reports today after close with consensus around $1.38 EPS on roughly $5.70 billion revenue. The memory capex inflection — HBM3e/4 and advanced packaging — is the read the committee is watching. UBS trimmed QCOM’s price target to $150 citing rising memory prices as a chip-stock headwind; LRCX guidance will indicate whether that pressure is pricing demand or simply shifting margin capture across the supply chain.

Section 02

Geopolitics

Trump Extended the Ceasefire — But the Blockade Stayed, and Iran Walked
Late Tuesday, Trump reversed his Monday posture and announced an indefinite extension of the ceasefire at the request of Pakistani mediators, to remain in place until Iran submits a comprehensive proposal to end the war, per Al Jazeera and Axios. The extension, however, preserves the US naval blockade on Iranian ports. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi characterized the continued blockade as an act of war and a violation of the ceasefire, per Al Jazeera, and indicated Tehran would not negotiate while under coercive pressure. Vice President Vance’s scheduled Islamabad trip was cancelled, per CBS News. The architecture is a managed pause rather than an off-ramp: the shooting has stopped, the economic coercion continues, and the diplomatic track is in limbo. That structure is precisely what oil markets are pricing — Brent retreated overnight to approximately $98 after briefly breaching $100 intraday Tuesday, per CNBC.
UK Inflation Jumped to 3.3% — The First Clean Iran-War Data Print
UK CPI accelerated to 3.3% year-over-year, which Reuters characterized as showing the first material second-order effects of the Iran war on developed-market inflation. The release is the first clean data point connecting the conflict’s energy-supply disruption to a consumer price index outside the US. March US CPI already printed at 3.3% with gasoline up 21.2%; the UK print confirms the inflation leg of the conflict is now global, not regional. The implication for IOWN’s thesis is direct: the structural cost-push pressures underpinning the dividend sleeve’s pricing-power names (GPC, CL, CHD) and the energy sleeve (CVX, VLO, OKE) are visible in incoming data across the G7. This is why the rate-cut path has narrowed even as growth expectations have softened — central banks globally are now navigating an inflation regime that they did not forecast entering the year.
The Warsh Confirmation Added a Second Fed-Independence Data Point
Fed-chair nominee Kevin Warsh testified Tuesday that the president had never asked him to commit in advance to any interest-rate decision, and he rejected the suggestion that he would serve as a compliant partisan instrument, per CNN and CNBC. Warsh also pitched a framework overhaul that would reduce FOMC meeting frequency and revisit the inflation-targeting regime. Sen. Thom Tillis indicated he would block the nomination in committee pending the Powell investigation, which matters because Republicans hold a 12–10 advantage on Banking — one dissent stalls the process. The hearing pressured gold and duration assets (GLD –2.83%, TLT –0.55%, miners down 6%+) as the market read the testimony as directionally hawkish relative to the political pressure Warsh would face. The committee’s durable read: Fed independence remains contested, but the transition path is now longer and more uncertain than the market had priced coming into April.
