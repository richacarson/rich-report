Geopolitics

Iran’s Negotiating Track Cracked — Ghalibaf Reportedly Out After IRGC Intervention
Iranian parliament speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf reportedly resigned from Tehran’s US-negotiating team after intervention from the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps, per Israeli broadcaster N12 and subsequent coverage from Times of Israel and FXStreet. CNBC has not independently confirmed the report, and Iranian outlets have denied it, but oil markets priced the development immediately — BNO (Brent proxy) advanced 4.29% on the session, consistent with futures trading above the $105 handle per CNBC, as the market read the move as evidence that the IRGC is consolidating control over the negotiating posture. The structural implication is important: Ghalibaf was the civilian-conservative channel that Pakistani mediators had been working with. An IRGC-led negotiating team tightens the demand that the US naval blockade end before any substantive talks resume. For IOWN, the energy sleeve carries this directly — CVX gained 0.69%, VLO eased 0.23% from an already-stretched base, and CTRA added 2.19%. The elevated Brent handle appears to have established a higher trading range.
Israel Said It Was “Ready to Renew” — Defense Minister Katz Signaled Targets Are Marked
Israeli Defense Minister Israel Katz stated Thursday that Israel is “prepared to renew the war against Iran” and was awaiting a US green light, per Times of Israel, Jerusalem Post, and Euronews. Katz specified that the IDF had marked targets for a campaign that would aim at Iran’s energy and electricity infrastructure and senior leadership succession. Reports citing Israeli officials indicated a decision point in Washington was anticipated within days. If taken at face value, the posture is consistent with a late-week escalation window rather than a sustainable de-escalation path. The commodity reaction on Thursday — oil up more than 4%, gold actually softer at –0.97% on dollar strength, silver down 2.83% — suggests the market is pricing supply risk rather than a broad risk-off event. For IOWN’s positioning, the defense sleeve (GD –0.63%, LMT –4.62% post-miss) did not function as a hedge on the day; the energy sleeve did. That dispersion is the actionable signal.
The Fed’s April 28–29 Meeting Is Now Framed Against a Hotter Inflation Path
The Fed convenes next Tuesday–Wednesday with no cut priced and the first move still pushed to late 2026 per Tuesday’s Reuters poll. JPMorgan Research now expects rates to hold steady through the remainder of 2026, citing the war-inflation leg and resilient labor data. Thursday’s initial jobless claims printed at 214,000 vs. 212,000 consensus — a marginal uptick that still leaves claims near cycle lows and is consistent with a labor market that has not yet cracked, reducing the urgency for policy easing. S&P Global’s flash composite PMI rose versus March per the press release, driven by stock-building into supply concerns and price hikes — a data structure consistent with cost-push inflation rather than demand strength. The Warsh confirmation process remains stalled with Sen. Tillis conditioning his vote on closure of the Powell investigation, which CNBC noted could leave the position unconfirmed past Powell’s May 15 term end. For the committee, the path is directionally clearer: a higher-for-longer rate regime with stagflation tail risk, which rewards the pricing-power names in the dividend sleeve (ATO, NEE, CL, CHD, STLD) and penalizes the multiple-expansion trade that had been pulling Tech higher.
