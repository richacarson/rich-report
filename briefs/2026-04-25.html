Markets — Weekend Edition

Intel’s Earnings Ignite the Broadest Semiconductor Rally of the Year
Friday closed a week defined by the semiconductor sleeve. Intel reported Q1 results after Thursday’s close that exceeded expectations by the widest margin in years—non-GAAP EPS of $0.29 versus $0.013 consensus on revenue of $13.58 billion, per Yahoo Finance. The results validated x86 CPU demand for AI infrastructure and lifted the entire chip complex on Friday. AMD surged 13.91% to $347.81 after DA Davidson upgraded the stock to Buy, calling it the primary beneficiary of Intel’s confirmation that x86 remains central to the AI build-out. QCOM rallied 11.12% to $148.85. IOWN’s new Q2 additions were immediate contributors: CRDO gained 5.12% and VST added 4.78%. TSM rose 5.17%. Tech (+2.81%) was the dominant sector by a wide margin, with the S&P 500 closing at a fresh all-time high of approximately ~7,139. The Nasdaq surged 1.91%. Comm services (–1.58%) and healthcare (–1.41%) lagged. Fear & Greed improved to 31—continuing the steady recovery from the conflict’s single-digit lows.

Section 02

Geopolitics

Ceasefire Extended — But the Framework Remains Fragile
Trump extended the Iran ceasefire on April 21, citing a “seriously fractured” Iranian government and giving Tehran three to five days to engage in negotiations, per CNN and CNBC. The extension averted the feared April 21 expiry scenario that prior briefs identified as the week’s primary risk. Trump stated Friday that Iran would make an offer aimed at satisfying US demands, per Reuters. However, Iran’s Foreign Ministry denied that any meeting between negotiators is planned, and US negotiators heading to Islamabad have not been met by an Iranian delegation. The pattern persists: Trump signals progress while Iran denies engagement, yet the ceasefire holds in practice even as both sides accuse the other of violations. Turkey’s foreign minister indicated Turkey may consider a role in Hormuz demining after a deal is reached—the first concrete reference to a post-conflict Hormuz infrastructure framework.
Oil Continues to Drift Lower on Peace Progress
Brent crude is trading near $105, continuing its retreat from the $115 highs of late March. WTI fell 1.72% on Friday. Reuters characterized Friday’s session as “tech boosts US stocks to record close, oil dips on signs of Iran peace progress.” The energy sleeve has transitioned from its war-premium leadership to a consolidation phase: VLO, CVX, and OKE are no longer the daily leaders but continue to generate elevated revenue from the structural supply disruption that persists even as the geopolitical risk premium compresses. The IEA’s characterization of the conflict as the “biggest energy crisis in history” remains accurate regardless of the diplomatic trajectory—Hormuz transit has not normalized, and the physical infrastructure damage from strikes on Asaluyeh, Saudi refineries, and Gulf aluminum smelters will take years to repair.
