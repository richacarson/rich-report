Michigan Sentiment Printed a Record Low — The Consumer Cracked Even as Equities Re-Rated
The University of Michigan’s final April reading came in at 49.8, revised up from the 47.6 preliminary print but still the lowest level on record — below the financial crisis, the COVID trough, and the post-Ukraine inflation spike per the press release. One-year inflation expectations jumped to 4.7% from 3.8% prior, the largest one-month increase since April 2025; five-year expectations climbed to 3.5%, the highest since October 2025. The disconnect between equity records and consumer psychology is the clearest tell of the regime: the AI-led tape and the war-inflation transmission are running on parallel tracks. For the FOMC’s April 28–29 meeting, the inflation-expectations breakout reinforces the higher-for-longer path that markets are now pricing through late 2026 per CME FedWatch.
A weekly close at fresh records on AI leadership, with Michigan inflation expectations breaking out and Brent holding above $100, captures the regime cleanly: the physical-AI thesis and the energy-inflation thread are repricing together, not against each other. The physical world matters.
Section 02

Geopolitics

Witkoff and Kushner Head to Pakistan — The Negotiating Channel Reopens as the Ceasefire Window Narrows
The White House confirmed Friday that Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner are traveling to Islamabad on Saturday for what the administration is framing as “direct talks” with Iran, per CBS News and CNBC. Vice President Vance, who led the failed first round, will remain on standby in Washington. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi arrived in Islamabad late Friday and met with Pakistan’s foreign minister per Times of Israel. The signals from Tehran are mixed: an Iranian Foreign Ministry spokesperson stated “no meeting is planned” between Iran and the United States, with Pakistan to convey observations — per Al Jazeera. If taken at face value, Iran’s posture is consistent with a mediated rather than direct dialogue, which would prolong rather than resolve the standoff. The two-week ceasefire is set to expire over the weekend, which is what gives the Pakistan track its catalyst weight into Monday’s open.
The Strait of Hormuz Stays Shut — Brent Held Above $100 Despite the Negotiating Track
Brent crude settled Friday at roughly $105.30 per barrel, little changed on the session per The National, with WTI giving back 1.5% to $94.40 on the U.S. inventory build. The wedge between Brent and WTI — consistent with a supply premium concentrated in the global cargo trade rather than U.S. pipelines — widened on the week. Per CNBC, the Strait remained shut as both sides continued ship-seizure activity. The longer the corridor stays closed, the greater the cumulative economic cost to both sides — a structure that historically forces a resolution but does not prescribe its timing or terms. For IOWN’s energy sleeve (CVX, VLO, OKE, CTRA, CNX), the elevated Brent handle has held a higher trading range for a third consecutive week. The materials sleeve (FCX, AEM, NTR, STLD) continues to trade with the dollar/rate path more than with the conflict tape.
The FOMC Meets Tuesday-Wednesday — Powell’s Last Meeting Into a Hotter Inflation Path
The FOMC convenes April 28–29 with markets pricing approximately 99.5% probability of no change per Polymarket and roughly 94% per CME FedWatch. The federal funds target range remains 3.50–3.75%. The March dot plot showed a median of one cut for 2026, but seven committee members projected zero cuts — and the path of least resistance moves further hawkish given Friday’s Michigan inflation-expectations breakout, the war-driven energy shock, and resilient labor data (March payrolls 178K, unemployment 4.3%). This is Powell’s final FOMC meeting and press conference. The Warsh confirmation track remains stalled per prior coverage, with Senator Tillis conditioning his committee vote on closure of the Powell investigation. The risk-management implication is asymmetric: the meeting itself may be uneventful, but the press conference is the last opportunity for Powell to anchor the policy framework before the chair transition uncertainty lands. For IOWN, the higher-for-longer regime continues to favor pricing-power names in the dividend sleeve.
