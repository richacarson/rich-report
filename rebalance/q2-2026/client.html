01 What Changed & Why

The global landscape shifted meaningfully this quarter. Ongoing conflict in the Persian Gulf has disrupted energy and fertilizer supply chains, while U.S. manufacturing has entered a new expansion phase for the first time in over two years. We used this as an opportunity to realign both portfolios — reducing exposure in areas facing headwinds and adding to sectors positioned to benefit from these structural changes.

Every change was informed by six proprietary research reports, our sector rotation framework grounded in academic evidence, and technical analysis of all eleven S&P 500 sector ETFs. The following pages walk through each change and the reasoning behind it.

02 Dividend Portfolio Changes 2 exits · 2 adds

We exited two positions and added two replacements, shifting capital from healthcare (which was too overweight given the macro context) into energy and agricultural inputs.

SELL Matson (MATX)

Marine Shipping — Matson’s daily trading volume had become too thin relative to our portfolio size, creating unacceptable execution risk. This is a practical constraint, not necessarily a judgment on the business, though one could argue against owning a shipping company in a time as we are in now.

SELL Agilent Technologies (A)

Life Sciences — Agilent remains a high-quality company — our investment thesis has not changed. This was a portfolio construction decision driven by timing: our healthcare allocation had grown to 16%, while energy sat at only 4%. The geopolitical environment created an opportunity in energy that we decided to act upon, given the circumstances.

BUY Nutrien (NTR)

Agricultural Inputs — Nutrien is the world’s largest fertilizer company, producing all three major crop nutrients — nitrogen, potash, and phosphate — plus running a global network of farm supply stores. Based in Saskatchewan, Canada, it has essentially no exposure to Middle Eastern supply disruptions. The Iran conflict destroyed approximately one-third of the world’s ammonia production capacity. Ammonia is the base ingredient for nitrogen fertilizer, which is responsible for roughly half of global food output. Rebuilding these facilities takes 3–5 years, making this a structural supply shortage, not a temporary price spike.

BUY Coterra Energy (CTRA)

Oil & Gas E&P — Coterra is an oil and gas producer operating in two of America’s best basins: the Permian (oil) and the Marcellus (natural gas). It gives us upstream energy exposure — the company that actually drills and produces fuel — complementing Valero, which is a downstream refiner. The natural gas side is especially timely: Qatar’s LNG destruction removed 20% of global supply, and European gas prices are now trading at roughly 19 times the U.S. price.

03 Growth Portfolio Changes 3 exits · 4 adds

The growth portfolio moves from 24 to 25 holdings, filling the vacancy from an earlier removal of DOCU (AI Risk). We exited three positions and added four, with each trade driven by our proprietary screening framework and original research.

SELL Gold Fields (GFI)

Gold Mining — We held two gold miners (Agnico Eagle and Gold Fields). We only need one given the fact that we wanted to add a copper miner stock. Agnico Eagle scores significantly higher on our quality framework, so we retained it and replaced Gold Fields with a copper producer. This diversifies our materials exposure from gold-only to gold plus copper, capturing the structural copper demand story from data centers, electric vehicles, and grid modernization.

SELL FinVolution (FINV)

Financial Technology (China) — Elevated geopolitical and regulatory risk from US-China trade tensions, combined with management guiding revenue down 5–15% for the year. Capital redeployed toward domestic companies with clearer growth visibility.

SELL PDD Holdings (PDD)

E-Commerce (China) — Significant trade friction exposure combined with business practices that did not align with our values-driven investment approach. Our research identified material concerns that made continued ownership inconsistent with our philosophies. This was both a risk management and values-based decision.

BUY Freeport-McMoRan (FCX)

Copper Mining — Freeport is the world’s largest publicly traded copper miner, operating the massive Grasberg mine in Indonesia alongside major operations in Arizona and Peru. Copper is essential to nearly every major growth trend today: a single large AI data center consumes 15,000–50,000 tons of copper, electric vehicles require 3–4 times more copper than traditional cars, and the electrical grid is being rebuilt to handle rising demand. New copper mines take 10–15 years to develop, creating a structural supply gap.

BUY Credo Technology (CRDO)

Data Center Connectivity — Credo makes the tiny but critical chips that allow data to move between processors at extreme speeds inside AI data centers. As AI systems scale to hundreds of thousands of GPUs, the wiring between them becomes the performance bottleneck. Credo’s high-speed connection chips sit at exactly that chokepoint. Revenue has grown from $107 million to a $1.3 billion annual run rate in roughly three years.

BUY Vistra Corp (VST)

Nuclear & Gas Power Producer — Vistra owns and operates power plants — including the second-largest competitive nuclear fleet in the U.S. — that generate electricity and sell it into wholesale markets. American power demand is expected to grow 50% by 2040, driven by AI data centers, reshoring of manufacturing, and electric vehicles. Vistra has locked in 20-year power purchase agreements with Meta and Amazon for over 2,600 megawatts of capacity.

BUY Marvell Technology (MRVL)

Custom AI Silicon & Photonic Fabric — Marvell designs custom chips for the biggest cloud companies — Alphabet, Amazon, and Microsoft — to power their AI data centers. Three-quarters of its revenue comes from data centers, growing 47% year-over-year. Marvell also makes the industry-standard chips that convert data between electrical signals and light — critical as data centers transition from copper cables to faster fiber optic connections.

04 Market Context

Geopolitical Disruption: The Iran conflict, which began on February 28th, has shut the Strait of Hormuz — the narrow waterway through which roughly 20% of the world’s oil supply flows. The immediate effects have been severe: crude oil surged 57% in March, and Qatar’s liquefied natural gas export facility was destroyed, removing 20% of global LNG supply. Perhaps most significantly for food security, 30–35% of global ammonia production has gone offline, sending fertilizer prices up approximately 50% — the most significant disruption since World War II. For portfolios, this creates both risk and opportunity. Energy and agricultural input companies with safe, domestic supply bases are positioned to benefit, while companies dependent on global trade routes face meaningful headwinds.

Manufacturing Renaissance: The ISM Manufacturing Index has expanded for three consecutive months (readings of 52.6, 52.4, and 52.7) following 26 straight months of contraction — the longest contraction streak since the 2008–2009 financial crisis. Historically, when manufacturing recovers after a prolonged downturn, industrial and materials companies tend to significantly outperform the broader market over the following 12 months. This expansion is being driven by three forces: defense spending acceleration (a $924 billion defense authorization plus a $200 billion supplemental), massive AI data center construction, and reshoring of manufacturing back to the United States ($1.2 trillion in announced domestic capacity).

Sector Leadership Shift: Sector leadership has rotated decisively this year. Energy leads with a gain of roughly 39.5% year-to-date, followed by Utilities at 8.3% and Materials at 8.7%. Meanwhile, Technology has declined 11.3%, and several sectors including Financials, Consumer Discretionary, and Real Estate are in confirmed technical downtrends. Our portfolio adjustments reflect this shift — adding to sectors with positive momentum while maintaining disciplined exposure to long-term technology themes through our highest-conviction names.
